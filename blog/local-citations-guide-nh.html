Local Citations: The Complete Guide
for NH Businesses

By Alex · February 17, 2026 · 11 min read

A local citation is any online mention of your business name, address, and phone number (NAP). Google uses citations to verify that your business is real, located where you say it is, and trustworthy enough to recommend to searchers.

For NH businesses trying to rank in local search, citations are one of the top 5 ranking factors. Yet most businesses either ignore them entirely or have inconsistent information scattered across the internet.

This guide covers exactly where you need to be listed, how to build citations efficiently, and how to fix the inconsistencies that might be holding you back.

Why Citations Matter for Local Rankings

Think of citations like references on a job application. The more reputable sources that confirm your business details, the more Google trusts that information.

Citations affect your rankings in three ways:

  1. Verification. Google cross-references your information across multiple sources. Consistent NAP data = higher trust.
  2. Discovery. Some customers find businesses directly through directory sites like Yelp, Yellow Pages, or industry-specific directories.
  3. Authority. Citations from authoritative sources (BBB, Chamber of Commerce, industry associations) carry more weight than random directories.

According to multiple studies, NAP consistency across citations is one of the top factors for Google Maps rankings.

The Two Types of Citations

Structured Citations

These are formal business listings on directory websites where your NAP appears in a consistent, structured format. Examples: Google Business Profile, Yelp, Yellow Pages, BBB.

Unstructured Citations

These are mentions of your business on blogs, news articles, event pages, or social media — places where your NAP appears in running text, not a formal listing. Examples: a local newspaper article mentioning your business, a blog post recommending you, or a community event page listing sponsors.

Both types matter. Structured citations are the foundation; unstructured citations add depth and authenticity.

The Essential Citation Sources (Priority Order)

Tier 1: Must-Have (Do These First)

  1. Google Business Profile — the single most important listing. Optimize it fully.
  2. Apple Maps (Apple Business Connect) — powers Siri and Apple Maps. Free to claim.
  3. Bing Places — Microsoft’s equivalent. Powers Cortana and Bing searches.
  4. Yelp — massive authority with Google. Claim and complete your free profile.
  5. Facebook Business Page — Google crawls Facebook. Ensure NAP matches.

Tier 2: High Authority (Do These Next)

  1. Better Business Bureau (BBB) — strong trust signal. NH BBB covers all of New Hampshire.
  2. Yellow Pages (YP.com) — still has strong domain authority despite being less relevant to consumers.
  3. Foursquare / Swarm — powers many third-party apps and data aggregators.
  4. Nextdoor — increasingly important for local businesses, especially in NH communities.
  5. LinkedIn Company Page — professional services especially benefit from this.

Tier 3: NH-Specific Sources

These are especially valuable because they’re geographically relevant:

  1. NH.gov Business Directory — state government authority
  2. Local Chamber of Commerce — Manchester, Nashua, Concord, Portsmouth, Keene, and Dover all have active chambers
  3. NH Business Review — the state’s business publication
  4. Seacoast Online — major regional news site for the seacoast area
  5. Union Leader / NH Union — state’s largest newspaper
  6. Hippo Press — alternative weekly covering southern NH
  7. InkLink News — community news for the Monadnock region
  8. Keene Sentinel — local newspaper for the Keene area
  9. Concord Monitor — capital city’s newspaper
  10. Foster’s Daily Democrat — covers Dover/Rochester/Somersworth area

Tier 4: Industry-Specific Directories

The right directories depend on your business type:

  • Home Services: Angi (formerly Angie’s List), HomeAdvisor, Houzz, Porch, Thumbtack
  • Restaurants: TripAdvisor, OpenTable, Grubhub, DoorDash
  • Healthcare: Healthgrades, Zocdoc, Vitals, WebMD
  • Legal: Avvo, FindLaw, Justia, Lawyers.com
  • Automotive: CarFax, RepairPal, AutoMD
  • Real Estate: Zillow, Realtor.com, Trulia
  • Hotels/B&Bs: TripAdvisor, Booking.com, Hotels.com

How to Build Citations Efficiently

Step 1: Audit Your Current Citations

Before building new citations, find out what already exists. You might be surprised by how many inconsistent listings are out there.

Free methods:

  • Google your business name in quotes (“Your Business Name”) and check the first 3-4 pages of results
  • Google your phone number to find listings you may have forgotten
  • Google your address to find old or incorrect listings
  • Use Moz’s free listing check tool (moz.com/local) for a quick scan

Make a spreadsheet tracking: site name, your URL on that site, whether NAP is correct, and what needs fixing.

Step 2: Fix Inconsistencies First

This is more important than building new citations. Inconsistent NAP data actively hurts your rankings.

Common inconsistencies:

  • Old phone number on some listings
  • Abbreviated vs. full street address (“St” vs. “Street”)
  • Suite number present on some, missing on others
  • Different business names (“Joe’s Plumbing” vs. “Joe’s Plumbing LLC” vs. “Joe’s Plumbing and Heating”)
  • Old address from a previous location

The fix: Pick your canonical NAP format and make EVERY listing match exactly. No variations. See our NAP consistency guide for detailed instructions.

Step 3: Build Tier 1 Citations

Claim and complete the 5 must-have listings first. This takes about 2-3 hours total:

  1. Google Business Profile (if not already claimed)
  2. Apple Business Connect (business.apple.com)
  3. Bing Places (bingplaces.com)
  4. Yelp (biz.yelp.com)
  5. Facebook Business Page

For each: use your exact canonical NAP, fill out every available field, add your best photos, write a compelling description, and select accurate categories.

Step 4: Build Tier 2-3 Citations (5-10 per week)

Don’t rush this. Google gets suspicious if 50 new citations appear overnight. Build 5-10 per week for a natural growth pattern.

Focus on NH-specific sources first since they carry geographic relevance. Then move to national high-authority directories.

Step 5: Build Industry Citations

Add 3-5 industry-specific directories that are relevant to your business type. These carry extra weight because they demonstrate industry relevance.

Data Aggregators: The Shortcut

Four major data aggregators feed business information to hundreds of smaller directories:

  1. Data Axle (formerly Infogroup)
  2. Neustar Localeze
  3. Foursquare
  4. Factual (now part of Foursquare)

Submitting correct information to these four aggregators automatically propagates to hundreds of downstream sites. This is the most efficient way to build citation volume.

Services like Yext, BrightLocal, or Moz Local can manage aggregator submissions and monitor citations for you (typically $25-50/month).

How Many Citations Do You Need?

The answer depends on your competition. For most NH businesses:

  • Minimum viable: 20-30 citations (Tier 1 + Tier 2 + a few industry-specific)
  • Competitive: 40-60 citations
  • Dominant: 80-120+ citations

In less competitive NH markets (Keene, Lebanon, Laconia), 30-40 clean, consistent citations often put you ahead of most competitors. In Manchester or Nashua, aim for 50-80+.

Quality and consistency always beat quantity. 30 perfect citations outperform 100 inconsistent ones.

Citation Building Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using a PO Box as your address. Google wants a physical location. If you work from home, use your home address (you can hide it on GBP) or get a virtual office address.
  • Creating duplicate listings. Multiple listings for the same business on the same platform confuse Google. Check for existing listings before creating new ones.
  • Using tracking phone numbers. Don’t use different call tracking numbers on different directories. Use your main number everywhere for consistency.
  • Ignoring old listings. If you changed your name, address, or phone number, old listings with incorrect data are actively hurting you. Find and fix them.
  • Buying bulk citations from cheap services. $50 for 500 citations sounds great until Google penalizes you for spammy links from low-quality directories.
  • Forgetting to update after moving. When you change locations, update EVERY citation. This is critical and often overlooked.

Monitoring Your Citations

Citations aren’t a “set it and forget it” task. Check them quarterly because:

  • Directories sometimes change or reset information
  • Data aggregators may overwrite your corrections with old data
  • Duplicate listings can appear from scraped data
  • Reviews on citation platforms need responses

Set a quarterly reminder to spot-check your top 10 citations (Tier 1 + top Tier 2) and verify everything is accurate.

Citations and the Bigger Picture

Citations are one piece of local SEO. They work best in combination with:

  • Google Business Profile optimization — your most important “citation”
  • Reviews — especially on your citation platforms (Google, Yelp, Facebook)
  • Local backlinks — links from NH-specific sites carry more weight than directories
  • On-page optimization — your website needs to match your citation information
  • Schema markup — reinforces your NAP data for search engines

No single factor wins the local SEO game. Citations build the foundation, but you need the full picture to rank consistently.
