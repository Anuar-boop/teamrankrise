Local SEO for Multiple Locations:
How to Rank Each One

By Alex · February 17, 2026 · 10 min read

If your business operates in multiple cities — maybe a dental practice with offices in Manchester and Nashua, or a plumbing company serving Concord, Dover, and Portsmouth — you need a different SEO approach than a single-location business.

Done wrong, you end up with duplicate content penalties, confused Google listings, and none of your locations ranking well. Done right, each location ranks in its own city’s map pack.

The Two Multi-Location Scenarios

Scenario 1: Multiple Physical Locations

You have actual offices, stores, or service points in different cities. Example: a dental practice with offices in Manchester and Nashua.

This is the easier scenario. Each location gets its own Google Business Profile and location page.

Scenario 2: Service Area Business (No Physical Location)

You’re based in one city but serve multiple cities. Example: a plumber in Manchester who also serves Nashua, Concord, and Bedford.

This is trickier. You can’t create separate GBP listings for cities where you don’t have a physical address.

Google Business Profile Setup

For Multiple Physical Locations

  1. Create a separate GBP listing for each location. Each listing needs its own unique:
    • Physical address
    • Local phone number (not a shared 800 number)
    • Business hours (may differ by location)
  2. Use the same business name across all listings. Don’t add city names to your business name (e.g., “Smith Dental Manchester”) — that violates Google’s guidelines.
  3. Write unique descriptions for each location. Mention the specific neighborhoods, landmarks, and communities each office serves.
  4. Post location-specific updates. Each listing should have its own Google Posts about that specific location’s events, offers, and updates.
  5. Collect reviews for each location separately. Send customers to the review link for the specific location they visited.

For Service Area Businesses

  1. One GBP listing with a service area. Set your service area to include all cities you serve (up to 20).
  2. Hide your address if you don’t serve customers at your physical location.
  3. Don’t create fake listings. Creating GBP listings with virtual offices, PO boxes, or UPS Store addresses violates Google’s guidelines and will get suspended.

Website Structure for Multiple Locations

This is where most businesses get it wrong. They either create duplicate pages or don’t create location pages at all.

The Correct Structure

yoursite.com/                     (homepage)
yoursite.com/services/            (main services overview)
yoursite.com/manchester/          (Manchester location page)
yoursite.com/nashua/              (Nashua location page)
yoursite.com/concord/             (Concord location page)

Or for service-area businesses:

yoursite.com/                     (homepage)
yoursite.com/areas/manchester-nh/ (service area page)
yoursite.com/areas/nashua-nh/     (service area page)
yoursite.com/areas/concord-nh/    (service area page)

What Each Location Page Needs

This is critical. Each location page must have unique content — not just the same template with the city name swapped.

  • Unique H1: “[Service] in [City], NH” — e.g., “Emergency Plumbing in Manchester, NH”
  • Unique meta description: Different for each page, mentioning the city
  • Location-specific content: 500-1,000+ words unique to this location
    • Specific neighborhoods, streets, or areas served
    • Local landmarks (“Near the Mall of NH” or “Off Route 101”)
    • City-specific customer reviews or testimonials
    • Local regulations or considerations specific to that city
    • Driving directions or parking info
  • LocalBusiness schema with that location’s address, phone, and hours
  • Embedded Google Map centered on that specific location
  • NAP information (Name, Address, Phone) for that specific location

What NOT to Do

  • Don’t create thin doorway pages. Pages that are identical except for the city name are “doorway pages” — Google penalizes these.
  • Don’t create pages for cities you don’t actually serve well. If you’re in Manchester and create a page for “Plumber in Berlin, NH” (2 hours away), Google will figure it out.
  • Don’t use the same content across location pages. Google’s duplicate content detection is sophisticated. Each page needs genuinely different content.

Citations for Multiple Locations

Citations (directory listings) need to be set up for each location individually.

  • Create separate listings on Yelp, Yellow Pages, BBB, etc. for each physical location
  • Use the location-specific phone number and address — not headquarters
  • Link each citation to its corresponding location page on your website (not the homepage)
  • Keep NAP perfectly consistent across all citations for each location

Content Strategy for Multi-Location SEO

Blog Posts

Write blog posts that mention specific locations naturally:

  • “5 Common Plumbing Issues in Older Manchester Homes”
  • “Why Nashua Homeowners Are Switching to Tankless Water Heaters”
  • “Concord’s Hard Water: What It Means for Your Pipes”

This creates unique, location-relevant content that supports your location pages without being doorway content.

Reviews Strategy

Each location needs its own review strategy:

  • Send customers to the GBP review link for the location they visited
  • Display location-specific reviews on each location page
  • Respond to reviews from the correct location’s account
  • Mention the city in your review responses

Common Multi-Location SEO Mistakes

  1. Using a single GBP for all locations. Each physical location deserves its own listing.
  2. Creating GBP listings without real addresses. Virtual offices and PO boxes get suspended.
  3. Identical location pages. The #1 mistake. Each page needs 500+ words of genuinely unique content.
  4. Linking all citations to the homepage. Each citation should link to its location’s specific page.
  5. Ignoring reviews for smaller locations. Even your secondary locations need steady review growth.
  6. Using one phone number for all locations. Each location should have a local number for that area.
  7. Not tracking rankings per location. You need to monitor rankings in each city separately.

Multi-Location SEO Checklist

Use this with your overall SEO checklist:

  • Separate GBP listing for each physical location
  • Unique phone number per location
  • Individual location pages with 500+ unique words each
  • LocalBusiness schema per location page
  • Embedded map per location page
  • Separate citation sets per location
  • Location-specific review collection
  • City-specific blog content
  • Per-location rank tracking
  • Consistent NAP per location across all platforms
