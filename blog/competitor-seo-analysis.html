How to Analyze Your Competitors’ SEO
Strategy (Free Tools)

By Alex · February 17, 2026 · 9 min read

Your competitors are ranking above you for a reason. They’re not lucky — they’re doing something you’re not. The good news: you can figure out exactly what that is and use it to build a better strategy.

You don’t need expensive software. Everything in this guide can be done with free tools and a bit of detective work.

Step 1: Identify Your Real Competitors

Your SEO competitors aren’t always who you think. The business down the street might be your offline competitor, but your SEO competitor is whoever ranks when you want to rank.

How to find them:

  1. Search your top 5 target keywords (e.g., “plumber Manchester NH”, “HVAC repair Nashua”)
  2. Note the top 3-5 businesses that consistently appear
  3. Check the Google Maps pack too — map competitors may differ from organic competitors
  4. Look at both local businesses AND national directories (Yelp, HomeAdvisor, Angi) that outrank you

Create a spreadsheet with your top 3-5 SEO competitors and their domains.

Step 2: Analyze Their Google Business Profile

For local SEO, this is the most revealing analysis you can do. Search your main keyword and look at the competitors in the map pack.

What to check:

  • Review count and rating. How many reviews do they have? What’s their average? How recent are their reviews?
  • Categories. Click on their profile and check their business categories (primary + secondary). Are they using categories you’re missing?
  • Posts. Are they publishing Google Posts regularly? What kind of content?
  • Photos. How many photos? Are they professional? Do they show their team, workspace, and results?
  • Q&A section. Have they answered common questions? This is often overlooked.
  • Services listed. What services do they list? Are they more detailed than yours?

If a competitor has 85 reviews and you have 12, that tells you exactly where to focus. See our GBP optimization guide and review strategy guide.

Step 3: Audit Their Website

Content Analysis (10 minutes)

Visit their website and note:

  • How many pages do they have? Count service pages, location pages, blog posts. A site with 50 pages has more ranking opportunities than one with 5.
  • Do they have location pages? Pages targeting “Concord,” “Dover,” “Portsmouth” separately? If they do and you don’t, they’re likely outranking you in those cities.
  • How deep is their service content? Do they have a paragraph about each service or a full dedicated page with 800-1500 words?
  • Do they have a blog? How many posts? How often do they publish? What topics?
  • Do they have schema markup? View source and search for “application/ld+json”.

Technical Quick Check (5 minutes)

  • Page speed: Run their URL through PageSpeed Insights. Are they faster than you?
  • Mobile experience: Open their site on your phone. Is it better than yours?
  • SSL: Do they have https? (Should be standard by now.)
  • Site structure: Is their navigation cleaner? Can you find what you need in 2 clicks?

On-Page SEO (5 minutes per page)

For their top-ranking pages, right-click and “View Page Source” to check:

  • Title tag: What exact keywords are they targeting?
  • Meta description: How are they framing their pitch?
  • H1 tag: Does it match the title or target different keywords?
  • Internal links: How are they linking between pages?
  • Content length: How many words on the page? (Rough estimate is fine.)

Step 4: Check Their Backlinks (Free Tools)

Backlinks are the hardest thing to replicate, which makes them the most valuable competitive intelligence.

Free Backlink Tools

  • Ahrefs Webmaster Tools (free for your own site, limited for competitors)
  • Moz Link Explorer (10 free queries/month per account)
  • Ubersuggest (3 free searches/day)
  • Google: link:competitor.com — shows some linking pages

What to look for:

  • Total referring domains. Not just total backlinks (one site can link multiple times).
  • Quality sources. Are they getting links from local newspapers, Chambers of Commerce, industry associations? These are the links that matter most for local SEO.
  • Content that earns links. Which of their pages have the most backlinks? These are the types of content you should create.
  • Directories/citations. Which directories are they listed in that you’re not?

Step 5: Monitor Their Rankings

Track where you and your competitors rank for the same keywords over time.

Free method: Create a spreadsheet with your target keywords. Once a month, search each keyword (in an incognito window) and record positions for yourself and top 3 competitors.

Semi-free tools:

  • Google Search Console — shows your own rankings (not competitors’)
  • Ubersuggest — limited free tracking
  • SERPWatcher by Mangools — affordable paid option ($29/month)

If a competitor suddenly jumps in rankings, investigate what changed. New content? New backlinks? GBP updates? Tracking lets you catch these patterns.

Step 6: Find the Gaps

The most valuable insight from competitor analysis is finding the gaps — things they’re doing that you’re not, and things you could do better.

Content Gaps

  • Topics they cover that you don’t
  • City/location pages you’re missing
  • FAQ content they answer that you don’t
  • Blog posts on topics your customers search for

Technical Gaps

  • Their site is faster than yours
  • They have schema markup, you don’t
  • Their mobile experience is better
  • They have proper internal linking

Authority Gaps

  • They have more/better reviews
  • They’re listed in directories where you’re not
  • They have links from local sources you could also get
  • Their NAP consistency is better

Turning Analysis Into Action

Analysis is useless without action. Here’s how to prioritize:

Quick Wins (Do This Week)

  1. Claim any directories where competitors are listed but you’re not
  2. Optimize your GBP to match or exceed competitor detail level
  3. Fix any technical issues (speed, mobile, schema) where competitors beat you

Medium Term (Next 1-3 Months)

  1. Create location pages for cities competitors are targeting
  2. Write content covering topics competitors rank for that you don’t
  3. Start a review acquisition campaign to close the review gap

Long Term (3-12 Months)

  1. Build backlinks from the same quality sources your competitors have
  2. Create better, more detailed content than your competitors on key topics
  3. Develop a consistent publishing schedule (competitors eventually stop)

What Most NH Businesses Get Wrong

  • Copying instead of improving. Don’t create the same page as your competitor. Create a better one. More detailed, more helpful, more local.
  • Analyzing once, then forgetting. Check competitors quarterly. The landscape changes.
  • Focusing on the wrong competitors. Analyze businesses that rank where you want to rank, not just businesses you know personally.
  • Getting discouraged. A competitor with 200 reviews and 100 backlinks didn’t build that overnight. They started where you are now. Consistency wins.
