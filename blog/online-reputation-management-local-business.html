Reputation

Online Reputation Management for Local Businesses

Alex Feb 17, 2026 10 min read

One bad Google review can cost you $30,000 in lost revenue over a year. One viral complaint on social media can undo years of trust-building. Your online reputation is the most valuable — and most fragile — marketing asset your business has.

This guide covers how to monitor what people are saying about your business, protect your reputation from damage, and actively build a positive online presence that drives customer trust and revenue.

Why Reputation Management Matters More Than Ever

The numbers tell the story:

  • 93% of consumers say online reviews influence their buying decisions
  • 87% won't consider a business with less than 3 stars
  • 94% say a negative review has convinced them to avoid a business
  • A one-star increase on Yelp leads to a 5-9% increase in revenue
  • Businesses with 4.0-4.5 stars earn 28% more revenue than those below 4.0

Your reputation directly impacts your Local Pack ranking too. Reviews are the third most important ranking factor — meaning a strong reputation doesn't just build trust, it makes you more visible.

The 5 Pillars of Reputation Management

1. Monitor — Know What's Being Said

You can't manage what you don't see. Set up monitoring for:

  • Google Reviews — Check your Google Business Profile daily
  • Yelp, Facebook, BBB — Check weekly
  • Industry-specific sites — Healthgrades, Angi, Avvo, etc.
  • Social media mentions — Search your business name on Facebook groups, Nextdoor
  • Google Alerts — Set up free alerts for your business name

Free Monitoring Setup (15 Minutes)

  1. Go to google.com/alerts
  2. Create an alert for your exact business name (in quotes)
  3. Create an alert for your business name + common misspellings
  4. Set delivery to "as it happens" for email notifications
  5. Turn on Google Business Profile notifications in the GBP app

2. Respond — Never Leave Reviews Unanswered

Every review deserves a response — positive and negative. Here's the framework:

4-star positiveWithin 24 hoursThank them, acknowledge the gap, offer to improve
3-star neutralWithin 12 hoursThank them, ask for specifics, offer resolution privately
1-2 star negativeWithin 4 hoursApologize, take responsibility, move offline to resolve
Fake/spamWithin 24 hoursFlag for removal, respond professionally

Read our detailed guides on responding to Google reviews and handling negative reviews.

3. Generate — Build a Steady Stream of Positive Reviews

The best defense against negative reviews is a mountain of positive ones. A single 1-star review against 200 5-star reviews barely moves the needle.

  • Ask every satisfied customer for a review (in person, email, text)
  • Make it easy — send a direct Google review link
  • Time it right — ask when satisfaction is highest (right after a compliment)
  • Follow up once — a gentle reminder 3 days later if they haven't left one
  • Never offer incentives for reviews (against Google's guidelines)

Our guides: getting more reviews and the first 50 reviews playbook.

4. Protect — Prevent Reputation Damage

Prevention is cheaper than recovery. Protect your reputation by:

  • Delivering consistently — Most negative reviews come from genuinely bad experiences
  • Setting expectations — Underpromise, overdeliver. Mismatched expectations cause complaints.
  • Following up proactively — Call after service to check satisfaction before they leave a review
  • Training your team — Every employee interaction is a potential review
  • Handling complaints immediately — An unhappy customer who gets resolved rarely leaves a negative review

5. Improve — Use Feedback to Get Better

Your reviews are free market research. Look for patterns:

  • Multiple complaints about wait times? Fix your scheduling.
  • Praise for a specific employee? Recognize them and replicate what they do.
  • Questions about pricing? Add clearer pricing to your website.
  • Mentions of competitors? Study what they're doing differently.

The businesses that treat reviews as feedback (not just marketing) are the ones that actually improve. Improvement leads to more positive reviews, which leads to more customers — a virtuous cycle.

Platform-by-Platform Strategy

Google Reviews (Priority #1)

Google Reviews directly impact your Local Pack ranking. This is where most customers look first.

  • Target: 4.5+ star average with steady monthly flow
  • Respond to 100% of reviews
  • Use GBP Posts to share positive reviews

Yelp

Yelp has strict review filtering. Don't ask for Yelp reviews directly (they filter those out). Instead, deliver great service and let Yelp reviews come naturally.

  • Claim and complete your Yelp business page
  • Respond to every review (especially negatives)
  • Add photos and business info

Facebook

Facebook Recommendations (the thumbs up/down system) are less impactful than Google but still visible. More importantly, Facebook is where local word-of-mouth happens.

  • Turn on Recommendations on your Facebook page
  • Be active in local Facebook groups (helpful, not promotional)
  • Respond to comments and messages promptly

Industry-Specific Sites

Claim your profile and respond to reviews on whatever platform your industry uses:

  • Home services: Angi, HomeAdvisor, Houzz
  • Medical: Healthgrades, Zocdoc, Vitals
  • Legal: Avvo, FindLaw, Lawyers.com
  • Automotive: CarFax, RepairPal
  • Restaurants: TripAdvisor, OpenTable

Handling a Reputation Crisis

Sometimes things go wrong — a viral complaint, a wave of negative reviews, or unfair media coverage. Here's the crisis playbook:

Step 1: Don't Panic (and Don't Delete)

Your first instinct will be to delete, deny, or fight back. Resist all three. Deleting comments looks like a cover-up. Denial escalates. Fighting back creates entertainment for observers.

Step 2: Acknowledge Publicly

Respond publicly with empathy and accountability. Use this template:

  • "Thank you for bringing this to our attention."
  • "We take this seriously and are looking into it."
  • "We'd like to make this right. Please contact [name] at [phone/email]."

Step 3: Resolve Privately

Move the conversation offline. Call the customer, listen, and offer a genuine resolution. Most angry customers just want to be heard.

Step 4: Follow Up Publicly

After resolution, add a public follow-up: "We reached out to this customer and resolved the issue. We've also [specific improvement you made] to prevent this in the future."

Step 5: Dilute with Positivity

After resolving the crisis, accelerate your positive review collection. The best antidote to negative press is an overwhelming volume of positive experiences.

Reputation Management for NH Businesses

New Hampshire has some unique characteristics that affect reputation management:

  • Small communities — Word travels fast in NH towns. One great experience (or bad one) gets shared quickly in local circles, Facebook groups, and Nextdoor.
  • Seasonal fluctuations — Tourism-dependent businesses (lakes region, ski country) see review spikes in season. Make sure you're prepared for peak periods.
  • Loyalty matters — NH residents value relationships. A business that responds personally to every review builds community loyalty that competitors can't buy.
  • Local media — NH Business Review, Union Leader, and local community papers are all potential amplifiers of both positive and negative stories.

In a state this small, your reputation IS your marketing. Building trust online translates directly to real-world word-of-mouth.

The Weekly Reputation Routine (30 Minutes)

Reputation management doesn't require hours per week. Here's a 30-minute weekly routine:

  1. Monday (10 min): Check and respond to all new Google reviews
  2. Monday (5 min): Check Yelp, Facebook, and industry sites for new reviews
  3. Wednesday (5 min): Review Google Alerts for any new mentions
  4. Friday (10 min): Send review request emails/texts to this week's customers

That's it. Consistency beats intensity. A business that spends 30 minutes every week on reputation will outperform one that does a 10-hour crisis management session once a year.

Common Reputation Mistakes

  1. Buying fake reviews — Google detects and removes them. You can also get your entire listing suspended. Never worth it.
  2. Ignoring negative reviews — Silence looks like you don't care
  3. Getting defensive in responses — Never argue with a reviewer publicly
  4. Only asking happy customers — Ask everyone; unhappy customers who feel heard often change their review
  5. Set-it-and-forget-it — Reputation needs ongoing attention, not one-time setup
  6. Obsessing over one bad review — Focus on the overall trend, not individual reviews

Bottom Line

Your online reputation is built one interaction at a time — and it compounds. Every positive review makes the next one easier to get. Every prompt response shows future customers you care. Every resolved complaint demonstrates integrity.

The formula is simple: deliver great service → ask for reviews → respond to everything → learn from feedback → improve. Do this consistently, and your reputation becomes a competitive advantage that's nearly impossible for competitors to replicate.

Start today: check your Google reviews, respond to any you've missed, and send a review request to your last 5 customers. That's 15 minutes that could change your business.
