How to Track Your SEO Results
(Without Being an Expert)

By Alex · February 17, 2026 · 10 min read

You’re paying for SEO. Your agency sends monthly reports full of charts and numbers. But you still can’t answer the most basic question: is it actually working?

You’re not alone. Most NH business owners we talk to say the same thing: they don’t know what to look at, what the numbers mean, or what “good” looks like.

This guide fixes that. We’ll show you the 7 metrics that actually matter, the free tools to track them, and what realistic progress looks like at 3, 6, and 12 months.

Why Most SEO Reports Are Useless

Here’s the problem with typical agency reports:

  • Too much data, too little insight — 30 pages of charts that don’t answer “am I getting more customers?”
  • Vanity metrics — they highlight “impressions” and “domain authority” instead of actual leads
  • No benchmarks — they show numbers but never say whether those numbers are good or bad
  • Jargon overload — CTR, SERP, DA, DR, backlinks… it feels designed to confuse

A good report should answer three questions in 30 seconds: Am I getting more visibility? Am I getting more traffic? Am I getting more customers?

The 7 Metrics That Actually Matter

1. Google Business Profile Views

This is the single most important metric for local businesses. Your Google Business Profile shows up in Maps, the local pack, and “near me” searches.

Where to find it: Go to your Google Business Profile dashboard → Performance. Look at “Business Profile interactions” — that includes calls, direction requests, and website clicks.

What good looks like:

  • Month 1-3: 10-30% increase in profile views
  • Month 3-6: 30-80% increase over your starting point
  • Month 6-12: 2-4x your starting numbers

Red flag: If GBP views are flat after 3 months of SEO work, something is wrong.

2. Organic Website Traffic

This is how many people find your website through Google (not ads, not social media, not direct). It’s the clearest signal that your website optimization is working.

Where to find it: Google Analytics 4 → Reports → Acquisition → Traffic acquisition. Filter to “Organic Search.”

What good looks like for local local businesses:

  • Starting point: 50-200 organic visits/month (typical for an unoptimized small business site)
  • After 6 months of SEO: 300-800 organic visits/month
  • After 12 months: 800-2,000+ organic visits/month

Don’t panic about day-to-day fluctuations. Look at the month-over-month trend. SEO takes time — expect a slow ramp for the first 2-3 months.

3. Keyword Rankings

Where you show up when people search for your services. This is the metric most people think of when they think “SEO.”

Where to find it: Google Search Console → Performance → Queries. This shows every search term where your site appeared in results, plus your average position.

What to track:

  • Money keywords: “[your service] + [your city]” (e.g., “plumber Manchester NH”)
  • Near-me keywords: “[your service] near me”
  • Question keywords: “how much does [service] cost in NH”

What good looks like:

  • Position 50+ → you’re essentially invisible
  • Position 10-20 → page 2 (almost nobody clicks here)
  • Position 4-10 → bottom of page 1 (some clicks)
  • Position 1-3 → the sweet spot (65%+ of all clicks)

Watch for keywords moving from page 2+ into the top 10. That’s the transition that creates real traffic. For local rankings, check our Google Maps ranking guide.

4. Phone Calls and Form Submissions

Traffic means nothing if it doesn’t turn into customers. Track every lead that comes from your website.

Where to find it:

  • Phone calls: Google Business Profile tracks “calls” automatically. For website calls, set up Google Analytics event tracking on your click-to-call link.
  • Form submissions: Track form completions in GA4 as conversions, or simply count emails from your contact form.

What good looks like:

  • Conversion rate of 3-8% from organic traffic (meaning 3-8 out of 100 organic visitors contact you)
  • For a local service business getting 500 organic visits/month: 15-40 leads/month from organic

If traffic is growing but leads aren’t, your website has a conversion problem, not an SEO problem.

5. Local Pack Appearances

The “local pack” (or “map pack”) is the 3-business box with a map that appears at the top of local searches. Being in this box is worth more than the #1 organic result for most local businesses.

Where to find it: Google Search Console shows impressions from “Maps” results separately. You can also manually search your key terms and see if you appear.

What good looks like:

  • Appearing in the map pack for 5-10 of your target keywords within 6 months
  • Appearing for 15-25+ keywords within 12 months

The three biggest factors for map pack rankings: NAP consistency, Google reviews, and proximity to the searcher. You can control the first two.

6. Review Count and Rating

Reviews directly impact your rankings and your conversion rate. Track them as an SEO metric, not just a reputation metric.

Where to find it: Your Google Business Profile shows your total review count and average rating. Check it monthly.

What good looks like:

  • Minimum: 20+ reviews with 4.2+ stars to be competitive in NH
  • Good: 50+ reviews with 4.5+ stars
  • Great: 100+ reviews with 4.7+ stars
  • Monthly velocity: 2-4 new reviews/month for a small business

7. Backlink Growth

Backlinks (other websites linking to yours) are one of Google’s top ranking factors. You should see steady growth if your SEO agency is doing its job.

Where to find it: Google Search Console → Links → External links. For more detail, free tools like Ahrefs Webmaster Tools or Moz’s Link Explorer show your backlink profile.

What good looks like for a NH local business:

  • Starting point: 5-20 referring domains
  • After 6 months: 30-60 referring domains
  • After 12 months: 60-120 referring domains

Quality matters more than quantity. One link from NH Business Review or your local Chamber of Commerce is worth more than 50 links from random directories. See our backlink building guide for strategies.

Free Tools You Need (and Nothing Else)

You don’t need expensive software. These four free tools cover everything:

Google Search Console (Free)

  • See which keywords bring people to your site
  • Track your average ranking position
  • Find technical errors Google detected
  • Monitor backlinks

Setup time: 5 minutes. Go to search.google.com/search-console, add your website, verify with a DNS record or HTML tag.

Google Analytics 4 (Free)

  • Track organic traffic volume and trends
  • See which pages get the most visits
  • Monitor form submissions and calls as conversions
  • Understand user behavior (time on page, bounce rate)

Setup time: 10 minutes. Install the GA4 tracking code on your website (one snippet in the <head> of every page).

Google Business Profile Dashboard (Free)

  • Track profile views, searches, and actions
  • Monitor review count and rating
  • See photos viewed and posts engagement
  • Track direction requests and calls

Setup time: Already done if you’ve claimed your GBP listing.

Google PageSpeed Insights (Free)

  • Check your Core Web Vitals scores
  • Get specific recommendations to speed up your site
  • Test both mobile and desktop performance

Setup time: None. Just go to pagespeed.web.dev and enter your URL.

Your Monthly SEO Check-In (15 Minutes)

You don’t need to check dashboards every day. Once a month, spend 15 minutes on this:

  1. Google Business Profile (3 min): Check profile views vs. last month. Note review count and rating. Respond to any new reviews.
  2. Google Search Console (5 min): Check total clicks and impressions for the last 28 days vs. previous 28 days. Look at your top 10 keywords — are positions improving?
  3. Google Analytics (5 min): Check organic traffic vs. last month. Look at your top landing pages. Note any form submissions or call conversions.
  4. Quick gut check (2 min): Are you getting more calls and emails from new customers than you were 3 months ago? That’s the ultimate test.

What Realistic Progress Looks Like

Here’s what we typically see for local businesses that invest in proper local SEO:

Month 1-2: Foundation

  • Technical issues fixed, schema markup added
  • GBP fully optimized
  • Rankings may actually dip slightly (normal after big changes)
  • Organic traffic: flat or small increase

Month 3-4: Early Signals

  • Keywords start moving from page 3-4 to page 2
  • Organic traffic up 15-30%
  • GBP views increasing
  • First map pack appearances for less competitive terms

Month 5-6: Momentum

  • Several keywords hit page 1
  • Organic traffic up 40-80%
  • Noticeable increase in calls and form submissions
  • Map pack appearances for 5-10 target keywords

Month 7-12: Results Compound

  • Multiple page 1 rankings for money keywords
  • Organic traffic 2-4x starting point
  • Steady lead flow from organic search
  • Reduced dependence on paid ads

Red Flags: When to Question Your SEO Agency

If any of these are happening, it’s time for a serious conversation with whoever is doing your SEO:

  • No movement after 4 months — some patience is needed, but zero improvement after 4 months of work means something is wrong
  • They can’t show you Search Console data — if they don’t have access or won’t share it, that’s a red flag
  • Traffic up but leads flat — they might be ranking for the wrong keywords or attracting irrelevant traffic
  • They guarantee specific rankings — no one can guarantee a #1 spot. Google’s algorithm considers 200+ factors. Here’s how to evaluate an SEO agency properly.
  • Reports are just screenshots — a real report explains what was done, what changed, and what’s next
  • They won’t give you account access — you should own your Google Analytics, Search Console, and GBP accounts. Always.

Common DIY Mistakes When Tracking SEO

  • Checking rankings daily. Rankings fluctuate. Daily checks create anxiety over normal variation. Monthly is enough.
  • Confusing paid and organic traffic. Make sure you’re looking at organic traffic only, not total traffic (which includes ads).
  • Ignoring mobile separately. Most local searches happen on mobile devices. Check your mobile traffic and rankings specifically.
  • Focusing on vanity metrics. Domain authority, total impressions, and number of indexed pages are interesting but don’t directly measure business results.
  • Not tracking before you start. Take a baseline snapshot before any SEO work begins so you can measure actual improvement.

The Only Question That Matters

After all the metrics, tools, and benchmarks, SEO success comes down to one question:

Are you getting more customers from Google than you were 6 months ago?

If yes, your SEO is working. If no, it’s time to figure out why — and the metrics above will tell you exactly where the problem is.

Most local businesses we work with see meaningful results within 4-6 months. The key is having the right strategy, executing consistently, and actually tracking progress so you know what’s working.
