NAP Consistency: The Simple SEO Fix Most NH Businesses Miss

By Alex · Feb 17, 2026 · 8 min read

There's one local SEO factor that sounds absurdly simple but trips up the majority of small businesses we audit: NAP consistency.

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone number. And if yours doesn't match exactly across every website, directory, and profile where your business appears, Google gets confused. Confused Google means lower rankings.

What Is NAP Consistency?

Your business information appears in dozens of places online — more than you probably realize:

  • Your website
  • Google Business Profile
  • Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn
  • Yelp, Yellow Pages, BBB
  • Apple Maps, Bing Places
  • Industry directories (Angi, HomeAdvisor, Healthgrades, etc.)
  • Local directories (NH Business Review, local Chamber of Commerce)
  • Data aggregators (Foursquare, Data Axle, Localeze)

NAP consistency means every single one of these listings shows the exact same business name, exact same address, and exact same phone number. Not "close enough." Exact.

Why Small Differences Matter More Than You Think

Here's what we mean by "exact." These are all different in Google's eyes:

  • "Bob's Plumbing" vs "Bob's Plumbing LLC" vs "Bob's Plumbing & Heating"
  • "123 Main St" vs "123 Main Street" vs "123 Main St, Suite 4"
  • "(603) 555-1234" vs "603-555-1234" vs "6035551234"

To a human, these are obviously the same business. To Google's algorithm, each inconsistency introduces doubt. And when Google has doubts about whether your business information is accurate, it's less likely to show you in search results — especially in the Google Map Pack.

How NAP Inconsistencies Happen

Nobody creates these inconsistencies on purpose. They happen naturally over time:

  • You moved offices — updated Google and your website, but forgot about Yelp, BBB, and 15 other directories
  • You changed your phone number — the old number still shows up on profiles created years ago
  • You incorporated — your legal name changed from "John's HVAC" to "J&J HVAC Services LLC" but not everywhere
  • Someone else created a listing — a data aggregator auto-generated a listing with slightly wrong information
  • Different employees set up different profiles — one used the street abbreviation, another spelled it out

A Manchester, NH HVAC company we audited had their business name listed three different ways across just 8 directories. Their phone number appeared in two different formats. Their address used "St" on some sites and "Street" on others. They had no idea — and couldn't figure out why they weren't ranking on Google Maps despite having great reviews.

The 20+ Places to Check (Checklist)

Here's your complete NAP audit checklist. Go through each one and make sure your information matches exactly:

Priority 1 — Fix These First

  • Your website (footer, contact page, about page)
  • Google Business Profile
  • Facebook Business Page
  • Yelp
  • Apple Maps / Apple Business Connect
  • Bing Places for Business

Priority 2 — Fix These Next

  • Better Business Bureau (BBB)
  • Yellow Pages (YP.com)
  • Angi (formerly Angie's List)
  • HomeAdvisor / Home Guide
  • LinkedIn Company Page
  • Instagram Business Profile
  • Nextdoor Business Page

Priority 3 — Fix for Maximum Impact

  • Foursquare / Factual
  • MapQuest
  • Local Chamber of Commerce directory
  • Industry-specific directories (Healthgrades for medical, Avvo for lawyers, etc.)
  • Data aggregators: Data Axle (InfoGroup), Localeze/Neustar, Foursquare
  • Any other site where your business appears

How to Find Your Listings

The fastest way to find where your business is listed:

  1. Google your business name — look through the first 3-4 pages of results and note every site that mentions you
  2. Google your phone number — this catches listings where your name might be slightly different
  3. Google your address — this catches listings where everything else might be wrong
  4. Check data aggregators — search for your business on Data Axle and Foursquare, as they feed data to hundreds of smaller directories

You'll likely find listings you didn't even know existed. Some were auto-created from phone books, others from data scrapers that pulled information from your website years ago.

How to Fix NAP Inconsistencies

Step 1: Decide on Your "Official" NAP

Pick one exact version of your business name, address, and phone number. Write it down. This is your canonical NAP. Every listing must match this exactly.

Tips:

  • Use the same name that's on your Google Business Profile — that's the one Google trusts most
  • Pick one phone format and stick with it: (603) 555-1234 is the most common format for NH businesses
  • Include suite/unit numbers consistently
  • Don't include "LLC" or "Inc" unless it's part of your Google Business Profile name

Step 2: Update Priority 1 Listings

Start with Google Business Profile and your website. These are the two most important. Make sure your website's footer, contact page, and schema markup all use the exact same NAP.

Step 3: Update Data Aggregators

This is the high-leverage move. Data aggregators feed information to hundreds of smaller directories. Fix your listing on Data Axle, Foursquare, and Localeze, and many of your smaller directory listings will auto-correct over time.

Step 4: Manually Fix Everything Else

Go through the checklist above. Claim each listing (many require verification), update the NAP, and save. This is tedious but essential. Budget 2-3 hours for a thorough cleanup.

Schema Markup: NAP for Search Engines

Beyond human-readable directories, you should also tell Google your NAP through structured data (schema markup) on your website. This is the LocalBusiness schema — a snippet of code that explicitly defines your business name, address, and phone number in a format Google's algorithm can parse directly.

If your website doesn't have schema markup, Google has to guess your NAP from the page text. With schema, you tell Google directly. Our SEO checklist covers how to add this to your site.

How Long Until You See Results?

NAP consistency improvements typically show results faster than most SEO work:

  • Google Business Profile updates: 1-7 days
  • Major directory updates: 2-4 weeks
  • Data aggregator propagation: 4-8 weeks for changes to cascade to smaller directories
  • Ranking improvements: Most businesses see measurable improvement in Map Pack rankings within 4-6 weeks

For context on broader SEO timelines, read our guide on how long SEO takes to work.

Real Impact: A Dover Electrician's Story

An electrician in Dover came to us frustrated. He had 47 five-star Google reviews, a fast website, and quality content. But he was stuck at position 5-6 in the Map Pack while competitors with fewer reviews ranked above him.

We ran a citation audit and found his business name listed differently across 12 directories. His old phone number still appeared on 6 sites. His address showed "Dover, NH" on some sites and "Dover, New Hampshire" on others.

After fixing every inconsistency — which took about 3 hours of manual work plus 2 data aggregator submissions — his Map Pack ranking improved from #5-6 to #2 within 5 weeks. Same reviews, same website, same content. The only change was making his NAP consistent everywhere.

Common Mistakes When Fixing NAP

  • Ignoring data aggregators — fixing individual directories without fixing the source means they'll revert
  • Inconsistent format on your own website — footer says one thing, contact page says another
  • Forgetting old phone numbers — set up a redirect or forward from old numbers to catch any remaining traffic
  • Creating duplicate listings — instead of updating an existing Yelp listing, accidentally creating a new one
  • Not monitoring — NAP drift happens over time, so check every 6 months
