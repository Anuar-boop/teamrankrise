Local SEO Glossary: 60+ Terms Every Business Owner Should Know

Alex Feb 17, 2026 15 min read

SEO is full of jargon. When your SEO agency says "we improved your DA, fixed crawl errors, and added schema markup," it can feel like a different language.

This glossary defines every local SEO term you're likely to encounter — in plain English. Bookmark this page and reference it whenever you need a quick definition.

A

Algorithm — The complex formula Google uses to decide which websites rank for which searches. Google updates this hundreds of times per year, with major updates having the biggest impact on rankings.

Alt Text — A text description added to images on your website. Screen readers read alt text for visually impaired users, and Google uses it to understand image content. Good for both accessibility and SEO.

Anchor Text — The clickable text of a link. "Click here" is bad anchor text. "Local SEO guide for local businesses" is good anchor text because it describes where the link goes. See our internal linking guide.

Authority — How trustworthy and credible Google considers your website. Built through quality backlinks, great content, and time.

B

Backlink — A link from another website to yours. Backlinks are one of the top 3 ranking factors. Quality matters more than quantity. See our backlinks guide.

Bounce Rate — The percentage of visitors who leave your site after viewing only one page. A high bounce rate can indicate your page doesn't match what people searched for (search intent mismatch).

Breadcrumbs — Navigation links showing the path from homepage to current page (e.g., Home > Blog > SEO Glossary). Helps users and Google understand your site structure.

C

Canonical URL — The preferred version of a page when multiple URLs show the same content. Prevents duplicate content issues.

Citation — Any online mention of your business name, address, and phone number (NAP). Directories like Yelp, Yellow Pages, and your Chamber of Commerce are citations. See our citations guide.

Click-Through Rate (CTR) — The percentage of people who click your listing in search results vs. how many saw it. If 100 people see your listing and 5 click, your CTR is 5%.

Content Marketing — Creating useful content (blog posts, guides, videos) to attract potential customers. See our content marketing guide.

Conversion — When a website visitor takes a desired action — calling you, filling out a form, making a purchase. The whole point of SEO.

Core Web Vitals — Three Google metrics measuring page speed and user experience: LCP (loading speed), INP (interactivity), and CLS (visual stability). See our speed guide.

Crawling — Google's process of discovering and reading web pages using automated programs called "crawlers" or "spiders."

D-E

Domain Authority (DA) — A third-party score (by Moz) from 1-100 predicting how likely a site is to rank. Not a Google metric, but useful for comparing sites.

Duplicate Content — The same or very similar content appearing on multiple URLs. Can confuse Google about which page to rank.

E-E-A-T — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness. Google's quality guidelines for evaluating content. Especially important for health, legal, and financial topics.

F-G

Featured Snippet — The answer box that appears above regular search results (position zero). Often read aloud by voice assistants.

Google Business Profile (GBP) — Your free business listing on Google that appears in Maps and local search results. Formerly "Google My Business." The most important local SEO asset. See our setup guide and optimization tips.

Google Maps 3-Pack (Map Pack) — The three business listings shown with a map at the top of local search results. Getting into this 3-pack is the primary goal of local SEO. See our Maps ranking guide.

Google Search Console (GSC) — Free Google tool showing how your site performs in search results — what queries you rank for, indexing issues, and more. See our GSC guide.

Google Analytics (GA4) — Free Google tool tracking website traffic — where visitors come from, what pages they view, and what actions they take. See our Analytics guide.

H-I

Header Tags (H1-H6) — HTML headings that structure your content. H1 is the main title (one per page), H2s are sections, H3s are subsections. Important for SEO and accessibility.

Impressions — How many times your page appeared in search results, whether or not anyone clicked.

Indexing — Google's process of storing your web pages in its database so they can appear in search results. If a page isn't indexed, it can't rank.

Internal Link — A link from one page on your website to another page on your website. Crucial for SEO and navigation. See our internal linking guide.

J-K

JSON-LD — A format for adding schema markup to your website. It's a code snippet that tells Google structured information about your business.

Keyword — A word or phrase that people type into Google. "Plumber Manchester NH" is a keyword. See our keyword research guide.

Keyword Cannibalization — When multiple pages on your site compete for the same keyword. This dilutes your ranking power instead of concentrating it.

Keyword Stuffing — Unnaturally repeating keywords on a page to try to rank higher. This is a spam technique that Google penalizes. See common SEO mistakes.

L-M

Landing Page — The specific page someone arrives on from a search result or ad. Should be optimized for conversion. See our landing page guide.

Local Pack — See "Google Maps 3-Pack" above.

Local SEO — SEO strategies focused on ranking in a specific geographic area. Essential for businesses serving local customers. See our complete local SEO guide.

Long-Tail Keyword — A specific, longer search phrase like "emergency plumber open sunday manchester nh." Less search volume but higher conversion rate.

Meta Description — The 155-character summary that appears below your page title in search results. Doesn't directly affect ranking but affects click-through rate.

Meta Title (Title Tag) — The page title that appears in browser tabs and search results. One of the most important on-page SEO elements. Should include your target keyword and be under 60 characters.

Mobile-First Indexing — Google primarily uses the mobile version of your website for indexing and ranking. See our mobile-friendly guide.

N-O

NAP — Name, Address, Phone number. Must be consistent across all online listings. See our NAP consistency guide.

Nofollow — An HTML attribute that tells Google not to pass ranking value through a link. Some links (sponsored, user-generated) should be nofollow.

Noindex — An HTML tag that tells Google not to include a page in search results. Used for pages you don't want people to find via search (admin pages, thank-you pages, etc.).

Organic Traffic — Visitors who find your website through unpaid search results (as opposed to paid ads). The goal of SEO.

On-Page SEO — Optimization done on your website itself — title tags, headings, content, internal links, images. See our SEO checklist.

Off-Page SEO — Optimization done outside your website — backlinks, citations, reviews, social signals.

P-R

Page Speed — How fast your web page loads. Faster pages rank better and convert more. See our speed optimization guide.

PPC (Pay-Per-Click) — Paid advertising where you pay each time someone clicks your ad. Google Ads is the most common form. See our SEO vs Ads comparison.

Ranking Factor — Anything Google considers when deciding where to rank a page. There are hundreds, but the top factors are content quality, backlinks, and user experience.

Responsive Design — A website that automatically adjusts its layout to fit any screen size (desktop, tablet, phone).

Rich Snippet — Enhanced search results that show extra information like star ratings, prices, or FAQ answers. Created using schema markup.

ROI (Return on Investment) — How much money you earn compared to how much you spend. See our SEO ROI calculator.

Robots.txt — A file on your website that tells search engine crawlers which pages they can and can't access.

S

Schema Markup (Structured Data) — Code added to your website that helps Google understand your content better. Results in rich snippets. See our schema guide.

Search Intent — What someone actually wants when they type a query. Matching search intent is critical for rankings and conversions. See our search intent guide.

SERP (Search Engine Results Page) — The page Google shows after you search. Includes organic results, ads, maps, featured snippets, and more.

Service Area Business (SAB) — A business that goes to customers instead of customers coming to it (plumbers, electricians, etc.). Has different GBP settings than a storefront.

Sitemap — An XML file listing all pages on your website. Helps Google discover and index all your pages. Submit it through Search Console.

SSL Certificate (HTTPS) — Security encryption that protects data between your website and visitors. Required for ranking — Google flags non-HTTPS sites as "Not Secure."

T-Z

Technical SEO — Behind-the-scenes optimization: site speed, mobile-friendliness, crawlability, schema markup, SSL, etc.

Title Tag — See "Meta Title" above.

Traffic — The number of visitors to your website. Can be organic (from search), direct (typed URL), referral (from other sites), or social (from social media).

URL Structure — How your page addresses are formatted. Clean URLs like /services/plumbing are better than /page?id=123&cat=4.

User Experience (UX) — How easy and pleasant your website is to use. Good UX = lower bounce rate = better rankings.

Voice Search — Searching by speaking to a device (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant). Growing rapidly and requires different optimization. See our voice search guide.

XML Sitemap — See "Sitemap" above.
