How to Write SEO-Friendly Content
That Actually Ranks

By Alex · February 17, 2026 · 10 min read

There’s a myth that SEO content means cramming keywords into every sentence until your page reads like it was written by a robot. That hasn’t worked since 2015, and Google’s Helpful Content System actively penalizes it.

Good SEO content is simply good content that’s structured so search engines can understand and rank it. Write for humans first, optimize for Google second. Here’s exactly how.

The Content Writing Process (Step by Step)

Step 1: Choose the Right Topic

Not every topic is worth writing about. The best topics for NH businesses sit at the intersection of three things:

  1. People search for it. Use Google’s autocomplete, “People Also Ask” boxes, and Answer the Public to find real questions.
  2. You can genuinely help. Write about what you know. Your expertise is your advantage over generic content farms.
  3. It connects to your services. A blog post should eventually lead the reader toward your business (naturally, not forcefully).

Topic ideas for NH local businesses:

  • “How much does [your service] cost in [city]?”
  • “Best [your industry] in [city] — what to look for”
  • “[Common problem] — how to fix it (and when to call a pro)”
  • “[Seasonal topic] for NH homeowners/businesses”
  • “[Your service] vs. [alternative] — which is right for you?”

Step 2: Research Before You Write

Before writing a single word, search your topic on Google and study the top 5 results:

  • What subtopics do they cover? (Make sure you cover the same ones, plus more.)
  • How long are they? (Match or exceed the content depth.)
  • What format do they use? (Lists, step-by-step, Q&A?)
  • What’s missing? (This is your opportunity to be better.)
  • Check “People Also Ask” boxes for related questions to answer.

Do a quick competitor analysis on the top results so you know what you’re up against.

Step 3: Create an Outline

Structure your content with clear headings (H2s and H3s) before writing. This serves two purposes:

  1. For readers: They can scan and find what they need. Most people don’t read top-to-bottom — they scan headings.
  2. For Google: Headings signal topic structure and help Google understand what your page covers.

Outline formula that works:

  • H1: Main topic (your title)
  • Introduction: Hook + what the reader will learn
  • H2: The problem or context
  • H2: The solution (main content)
  • H2-H3: Detailed sections with specific advice
  • H2: Common mistakes to avoid
  • H2: Summary or action steps
  • CTA: What to do next

Step 4: Write the Content

Opening (Make It Count)

Your first paragraph determines whether someone stays or leaves. Start with:

  • A problem they recognize (“Your website isn’t showing up on Google and you don’t know why.”)
  • A surprising stat (“93% of online experiences start with a search engine.”)
  • A direct answer (“Yes, local SEO is worth it. Here’s why.”)

Do NOT start with a generic intro like “In today’s digital landscape…” Nobody wants to read that.

Body (Be Specific)

  • Use concrete examples. Instead of “optimize your website,” say “add your city name to your title tag, like ‘Best Plumber in Manchester NH.’”
  • Include numbers. “68% of online experiences start with search” is more convincing than “most people use search engines.”
  • Write short paragraphs. 2-3 sentences max. Walls of text kill engagement, especially on mobile.
  • Use lists and formatting. Bullet points, numbered lists, bold key phrases. These improve readability and time on page.
  • Answer questions directly. If your heading is a question, answer it in the first sentence. Voice search rewards direct answers.

Closing (Clear Next Step)

End every piece of content with a clear action the reader can take. For NH service businesses, this is usually: “Get a free quote,” “Call us,” or “Read this related guide.”

Step 5: Optimize for SEO

After writing for humans, go back and make sure Google can understand your content:

Title Tag (The Most Important On-Page Element)

  • Include your target keyword near the beginning
  • Keep it under 60 characters
  • Make it compelling enough to click (“7 Proven Ways…” or “The Complete Guide…”)
  • Add your location if it’s a local topic (“… for NH Businesses”)

Meta Description

  • Summarize the page in 150-160 characters
  • Include the keyword naturally
  • Add a call-to-action (“Learn how…” or “Find out why…”)

Headings

  • One H1 per page (your main title)
  • Use H2s for major sections, H3s for subsections
  • Include variations of your keyword naturally in some headings

Internal Links

  • Link to 3-5 related pages on your site within the content
  • Use descriptive anchor text (not “click here”)
  • Link to relevant service pages, location pages, and blog posts
  • This is one of the most underused SEO strategies. See our content marketing guide for more on internal linking.

Images

  • Add relevant images (screenshots, diagrams, before/after)
  • Write descriptive alt text for accessibility and SEO
  • Compress images to maintain page speed

Content Length: How Long Should It Be?

There’s no magic number, but for ranking purposes:

  • Service pages: 500-1,000 words. Cover what you do, who you serve, your process, and a CTA.
  • Location pages: 600-1,200 words. Include local context, service details, and testimonials from that area.
  • Blog posts: 800-2,000 words. Long enough to be comprehensive, short enough to hold attention.
  • Pillar guides: 2,000-3,500 words. Comprehensive resources that cover a topic thoroughly.

The right length is whatever it takes to fully answer the searcher’s question. Don’t pad content to hit a word count. Don’t cut essential information to keep it short.

What Google Wants in 2026

Google’s Helpful Content System rewards content that demonstrates:

  • Experience. First-hand knowledge of the topic. Share your own work, results, and stories.
  • Expertise. Deep knowledge that goes beyond surface-level advice.
  • Authoritativeness. Recognition from others in your field (reviews, mentions, links).
  • Trustworthiness. Accurate information, transparent about who you are.

For NH businesses, this means: write about what you actually do, share real examples from your work, and don’t pretend to be something you’re not.

Common Content Mistakes to Avoid

  • Keyword stuffing. Using your keyword 50 times on a page. Google penalizes this. Use it naturally 3-5 times.
  • Thin content. Pages with 100-200 words that don’t say anything useful. Either expand them or remove them.
  • Duplicate content. Copying text from other sites or using the same content on multiple pages of your own site.
  • Writing for search engines, not people. If your content sounds robotic, people leave quickly (high bounce rate), which hurts rankings.
  • No clear purpose. Every page should answer a specific question or serve a specific intent.
  • Ignoring existing content. Sometimes updating an old page works better than writing a new one.

The Minimum Content Your NH Business Needs

  1. Homepage with clear value proposition and location signals
  2. Service pages (one per service, not everything on one page)
  3. About page with your story and credentials
  4. Contact page with NAP information
  5. Location pages for each city you serve
  6. 3-5 blog posts answering your customers’ most common questions

This gives you roughly 10-15 pages of content, which is enough to start ranking for your core keywords. Then add 1-2 blog posts per month to keep growing. Consistency over time is what wins.
