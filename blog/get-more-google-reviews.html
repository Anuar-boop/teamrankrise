Google Reviews

How to Get More Google Reviews for Your NH Business

Alex Feb 17, 2026 11 min read

Here's a stat that should make every NH business owner pay attention: 93% of consumers say online reviews impact their purchasing decisions, and Google reviews are the single biggest factor in local search rankings.

If your business has fewer reviews than your competitors — or worse, no reviews at all — you're losing customers every single day. The plumber in Manchester with 47 reviews will always get picked over the plumber with 3 reviews, even if the second plumber does better work.

The good news? Getting more Google reviews isn't complicated. You don't need fancy software. You don't need to bribe customers. You just need a system.

Here are 9 strategies that actually work for local businesses in New Hampshire.

Why Google Reviews Matter More Than You Think

Before we dive into the strategies, let's be clear about why this matters:

  • Local rankings: Google uses review count and quality as a major ranking factor for the Map Pack. More positive reviews = higher position = more visibility.
  • Click-through rate: Businesses with 4.5+ star ratings get 28% more clicks from search results than businesses with lower ratings.
  • Trust: 88% of consumers trust online reviews as much as personal recommendations. In a close-knit state like New Hampshire, that's huge.
  • Conversion: Reviews provide social proof. When someone Googles "best dentist in Concord NH" and sees you have 60+ positive reviews, the decision is basically made.

1. Just Ask (Most Businesses Don't)

This sounds obvious, but it's the single most underused strategy. 70% of customers will leave a review if you simply ask them. Yet most businesses never ask.

The best time to ask is right after you've delivered great service — when the customer is happiest. For a restaurant, that's when the server drops off the check. For an HVAC company, that's right after a successful repair. For a dentist, it's at checkout.

Keep it simple: "If you had a good experience, it would really help us if you left a Google review. We're a small local business and reviews make a big difference."

That's it. No pressure, no scripts, no awkwardness.

2. Create a Direct Review Link

The #1 reason customers don't leave reviews? It's too many steps. They have to Google your business, find your listing, scroll to reviews, click "Write a review"... and by step 3, they've given up.

Fix this by creating a direct link that takes customers straight to the review form:

  1. Go to your Google Business Profile
  2. Click "Ask for reviews" (or search "Google place ID finder")
  3. Copy the direct review link
  4. Shorten it with a service like bit.ly if needed

Now you have a one-click link you can put everywhere — emails, text messages, receipts, business cards.

3. Send a Follow-Up Email or Text

Timing matters. The best window for asking for a review is within 24 hours of the service. After that, the customer's enthusiasm fades and they move on with their day.

A simple follow-up email works great:

Subject: Thanks for choosing [Your Business]

Hi [Name],

Thanks for choosing us for [service]. We hope everything went great!

If you have a minute, a Google review would mean the world to us. It helps other [city] residents find us when they need [service].

[Leave a Review →] (link to your direct review URL)

Thanks,
[Your name]

Keep it short, personal, and include the direct link. Don't ask for a "5-star review" — that violates Google's policies and feels manipulative. Just ask for an honest review.

4. Add a Review Link to Your Email Signature

Every email you send is a chance to remind customers about reviews. Add a simple line to your email signature:

"Love our service? Leave us a Google review"

This works passively — you're not asking anyone directly, but the link is always there. Over months, this adds up to a steady trickle of reviews.

5. Put QR Codes in Your Physical Location

If you have a physical storefront, office, or service vehicle, QR codes are gold. Create a QR code that links directly to your Google review page and put it:

  • At your checkout counter or reception desk
  • On receipts or invoices
  • On business cards or thank-you cards
  • On your service vehicle (for contractors, landscapers, etc.)
  • In your waiting room

A simple sign that says "Enjoying your visit? Scan to leave a review" with a QR code is surprisingly effective. Customers can do it right there on their phone while they're still in your space.

6. Respond to Every Single Review

This one is critical and most businesses skip it. Responding to reviews — both positive and negative — signals to Google that your business is active and engaged. It also encourages more reviews because customers see that the owner actually reads them.

For positive reviews, keep it genuine:

"Thanks so much, Sarah! We loved having you in. Hope to see you again soon!"

For negative reviews, stay professional and take it offline:

"Hi Mark, we're sorry to hear about your experience. This isn't the standard we hold ourselves to. Please call us at [phone] so we can make this right."

Never argue with negative reviews publicly. Other potential customers are watching how you handle criticism.

7. Train Your Team to Ask

If you have employees, they need to be part of the review strategy. The person interacting with the customer at the point of service is in the best position to ask.

Make it easy for them:

  • Give them a simple script (see strategy #1)
  • Print cards with the QR code they can hand out
  • Recognize team members who generate reviews (gamify it)
  • Make it part of the checkout or wrap-up process

A dental office in Nashua we worked with went from 12 reviews to 85 in 6 months just by having the front desk ask every patient at checkout. No software, no incentives — just consistent asking.

8. Don't Ignore Other Platforms

While Google reviews should be your primary focus (they directly impact search rankings), don't ignore other platforms:

  • Yelp — still important for restaurants, salons, and home services
  • Facebook — many NH customers check Facebook recommendations
  • Industry-specific sites — Houzz for contractors, Healthgrades for doctors, Avvo for lawyers

A strong presence across multiple review platforms builds overall trust and local SEO authority.

9. Make It Part of Your Routine

The businesses that consistently get reviews don't run one-off campaigns. They build review collection into their daily operations.

Here's a simple weekly routine that works:

  • Monday: Send follow-up emails/texts to last week's customers
  • Wednesday: Respond to any new reviews
  • Friday: Check review count vs. top competitors — are you closing the gap?

Consistency beats intensity. Five reviews per month, every month, for a year gives you 60 reviews. That's enough to dominate most local markets in New Hampshire.

What NOT to Do (Google Will Penalize You)

A few things to avoid:

  • Never offer incentives for reviews. No discounts, no freebies, no contest entries. Google's policies explicitly prohibit this, and they will remove reviews (or worse, suspend your profile) if they detect it.
  • Never buy fake reviews. Google's algorithms are increasingly sophisticated at detecting fake reviews. The penalty is severe — profile suspension or removal.
  • Never ask only happy customers. Review-gating (filtering out unhappy customers before they review) violates Google's policies.
  • Never review your own business. Google knows. It's not worth the risk.

How Reviews Impact Your Local Rankings

Google's local algorithm weighs three main factors: relevance, distance, and prominence. Reviews are a massive part of prominence.

Specifically, Google looks at:

  • Review count: More reviews = stronger signal
  • Average rating: 4.5+ is the sweet spot
  • Review recency: Recent reviews carry more weight than old ones
  • Review velocity: A steady stream beats a sudden burst
  • Keywords in reviews: When customers mention your services or city naturally, it helps rankings

This is why review strategy is a core part of our local SEO approach. You can have perfect on-page SEO and a flawless Google Business Profile, but if your competitor has 3x more reviews, they'll likely rank above you.

Start Today: Your 5-Minute Action Plan

Don't overcomplicate this. Here's what to do right now:

  1. Create your direct review link (5 minutes)
  2. Ask your next 5 customers for a review using the simple script in strategy #1
  3. Respond to every existing review you haven't replied to yet
  4. Add the review link to your email signature
  5. Set a weekly reminder to follow up on reviews

If you do just these 5 things, you'll see more reviews coming in within the first week.
