Google Analytics for Local Businesses:
What to Track and Why

By Alex · February 17, 2026 · 9 min read

Google Analytics 4 (GA4) is free and tells you exactly where your website visitors come from, what they do on your site, and whether they become customers. Yet most local businesses either don’t have it installed or have never looked at it.

You don’t need to become a data analyst. You need to check 5 things once a month. Here’s the no-jargon guide to tracking what matters.

Setting Up GA4 (5 Minutes)

  1. Go to analytics.google.com and sign in with your Google account
  2. Click “Start measuring” and create a property for your website
  3. Add the tracking code to your website (Google gives you a snippet to paste in your <head> tag, or use a plugin if on WordPress)
  4. Verify it’s working: visit your own site, then check GA4 → Real-time — you should see 1 active user

That’s it for basic setup. Data starts flowing immediately.

The 5 Reports That Matter for Local Businesses

1. Where Your Traffic Comes From

GA4 path: Reports → Acquisition → Traffic acquisition

This shows how people find your website:

  • Organic Search: People who found you through Google/Bing (this is your SEO traffic)
  • Direct: People who typed your URL or clicked a bookmark
  • Referral: People who came from other websites linking to you
  • Social: People who came from social media
  • Paid Search: People who clicked your Google Ads

What to look for: Organic Search should be your #1 or #2 traffic source. If it’s not, your SEO needs attention. Watch for month-over-month trends — is organic traffic growing?

2. Which Pages Get the Most Traffic

GA4 path: Reports → Engagement → Pages and screens

Shows which pages people actually visit. For most local businesses:

  • Homepage should be #1 (if not, your brand search might have issues)
  • Service pages and location pages should appear in the top 10
  • Blog posts that rank well will show up (these are your content marketing wins)

What to look for: Your most important pages (services, contact) should be getting traffic. If they’re not, they may need better internal linking or keyword targeting.

3. Geographic Location of Visitors

GA4 path: Reports → User attributes → Demographic details → Filter by Region

For local businesses, this is critical. You want visitors from your service area, not random traffic from across the country.

What to look for: Majority of traffic should come from New Hampshire and nearby states. If you’re getting lots of traffic from California or India, your keywords may be too broad.

4. Conversions (The Most Important Metric)

GA4 path: Reports → Engagement → Conversions (after setup)

Traffic means nothing if visitors don’t become customers. Set up these conversion events:

  • Form submissions: Contact forms, quote requests, appointment bookings
  • Phone clicks: Clicks on your clickable phone number
  • Direction clicks: Clicks on your Google Maps embed
  • Chat interactions: If you have a chat widget

In GA4, go to Admin → Events → Create event to set up custom conversion tracking. Or use Google Tag Manager for more control.

What to look for: Your conversion rate (conversions ÷ visitors). Target 3-5% for local businesses. Below 2%? Your pages need optimization.

5. Mobile vs Desktop

GA4 path: Reports → Tech → Tech overview

Shows how people access your site. Most local businesses see 60-70% mobile traffic.

What to look for: If mobile conversion rate is much lower than desktop, your site has mobile usability issues. Fix them — that’s the majority of your potential customers.

Monthly Analytics Check-In (15 Minutes)

Set a calendar reminder for the first Monday of each month. Check:

  1. Total visitors vs last month. Growing, flat, or declining?
  2. Organic search traffic. Is it increasing? (Good SEO = steady growth)
  3. Top pages. Are your important pages getting traffic?
  4. Conversions. How many leads did the website generate?
  5. Mobile experience. Are mobile users bouncing more than desktop?

That’s it. 15 minutes, 5 checks, once a month. You’ll know more about your online presence than 90% of your competitors.

GA4 + Google Search Console

Link your GA4 property to Google Search Console for the complete picture:

  • Search Console tells you what people search to find your site (queries, impressions, click-through rates, average position)
  • GA4 tells you what they do after they arrive (pages visited, time on site, conversions)
  • Together: You see the full journey from search query to customer action

To link them: GA4 → Admin → Product links → Search Console links → Link. Both properties must use the same Google account.

Common Mistakes

  1. Not filtering out your own visits. You and your team visiting the site inflate numbers. In GA4, create an internal traffic filter under Admin → Data Streams → Configure tag settings.
  2. Obsessing over total pageviews. Pageviews without context are meaningless. A page with 100 visits and 10 conversions beats a page with 1,000 visits and 0 conversions.
  3. Ignoring bounce rate context. A high bounce rate on your contact page might be fine — they got your phone number and called. High bounce on your service page is a problem.
  4. Not setting up conversions. Without conversion tracking, you’re flying blind. Set up at least phone click and form submit events.
  5. Checking too often or too rarely. Daily checking leads to overreaction. Yearly checking means you miss problems for months. Monthly is right for most local businesses.
  6. Not connecting to Search Console. You’re only seeing half the picture without it.

What to Do With Your Data

Analytics is only valuable if you act on it:

  • Low traffic to service page? → Improve keywords, add more internal links, create supporting blog content
  • High traffic but low conversions? → Optimize the landing page (CTA, form, phone number)
  • Most traffic from outside your area? → Add more local signals, city keywords, location pages
  • Mobile users bouncing? → Fix mobile experience and page speed
  • Blog getting traffic but no leads? → Add stronger CTAs and links to service pages in blog posts
