Schema Markup for Local Businesses: The Hidden SEO Advantage

By Alex · Feb 17, 2026 · 10 min read

When we audit NH business websites, one issue comes up on almost every single one: no schema markup. It's the most overlooked SEO opportunity for local businesses — and one of the easiest wins.

Schema markup is code you add to your website that tells Google exactly what your business is, where it's located, what services you offer, your hours, your reviews, and more. Without it, Google has to guess by reading your page text. With it, you're handing Google a structured cheat sheet.

What Is Schema Markup? (Plain English)

Think of schema markup as a label system for your website. Instead of just having text that says "Call us at (603) 555-1234," schema markup tells Google: "This is a phone number, belonging to this business, at this address, that offers these services."

It's invisible to your visitors — they won't see any difference on your pages. But Google's crawler sees it clearly and uses it to:

  • Better understand what your business does
  • Display rich results in search (stars, hours, phone number, price ranges)
  • Connect your website to your Google Business Profile
  • Show your business information in knowledge panels
  • Improve voice search answers (Siri, Google Assistant, Alexa)

What Rich Results Look Like

When your schema markup is set up correctly, your search listing can show extra information that makes it stand out:

  • Star ratings — aggregate review scores displayed under your listing
  • Business hours — "Open now" or "Closes at 5 PM" shown directly in results
  • Price range — "$$" or "$$$" indicators for service businesses
  • FAQ answers — expandable Q&A directly in search results
  • Service list — your specific services shown below the listing
  • Breadcrumbs — clean navigation path instead of ugly URLs

Rich results get significantly higher click-through rates. A search result with star ratings and hours gets 20-30% more clicks than a plain text listing. That's more visitors from the same ranking position.

The 5 Schema Types Every Local Business Needs

1. LocalBusiness Schema (Required)

This is the foundation. It tells Google your business name, address, phone, hours, and what you do. Every NH business website should have this on the homepage at minimum.

Key fields to include:

  • name — your business name (match your Google Business Profile exactly)
  • address — full street address with city, state, zip
  • telephone — your primary phone number
  • openingHoursSpecification — your hours for each day of the week
  • priceRange — a rough price indicator ("$" to "$$$$")
  • areaServed — cities and regions you serve
  • serviceType — list of services you offer
  • image — your business photo or logo
  • url — your website URL

Use the most specific business type available. Instead of just "LocalBusiness," use "Plumber," "Dentist," "Restaurant," "LegalService," etc. Google has hundreds of specific types.

2. Service Schema

Add Service schema to each of your service pages. This helps Google understand exactly what you offer and can trigger service-related rich results.

A plumber's service page for "Water Heater Repair" should have Service schema with the service name, description, price range, area served, and a link back to the provider (your LocalBusiness schema).

3. Review/AggregateRating Schema

If you display customer reviews on your website, wrap them in Review schema. If you show an overall rating (like "4.8 out of 5 from 47 reviews"), use AggregateRating schema.

This is what triggers star ratings in search results — the gold standard of rich results. Getting stars in your listing is one of the highest-impact SEO wins available.

Important: Only use review schema for legitimate, first-party reviews. Don't use it for fabricated reviews or self-reviews — Google will penalize this.

4. FAQ Schema

If you have a FAQ section on any page, wrap each question-answer pair in FAQPage schema. Google may display these as expandable dropdowns directly in search results.

FAQ rich results take up significant screen real estate — your listing can visually dominate the search page. Common FAQ topics for local businesses:

  • "How much does [service] cost?"
  • "What areas do you serve?"
  • "Do you offer free estimates?"
  • "How quickly can you respond?"
  • "Are you licensed and insured?"

5. BreadcrumbList Schema

Breadcrumb schema tells Google about your site's navigation structure. Instead of showing a raw URL like "teamrankrise.com/blog/schema-markup-local-business.html" in search results, Google shows "Home > Blog > Schema Markup" — which is cleaner and more clickable.

Every page deeper than your homepage should have breadcrumb schema.

How to Add Schema Markup (3 Methods)

Method 1: JSON-LD (Recommended)

JSON-LD is the format Google recommends. It's a block of code you add to the <head> or <body> of your HTML page. It doesn't touch your visible content — it sits in a <script> tag.

Example LocalBusiness schema for a Manchester plumber:

<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Plumber",
  "name": "Bob's Plumbing Services",
  "telephone": "(603) 555-1234",
  "address": {
    "@type": "PostalAddress",
    "streetAddress": "123 Elm St",
    "addressLocality": "Manchester",
    "addressRegion": "NH",
    "postalCode": "03101"
  },
  "openingHoursSpecification": [
    {
      "@type": "OpeningHoursSpecification",
      "dayOfWeek": ["Monday","Tuesday","Wednesday","Thursday","Friday"],
      "opens": "07:00",
      "closes": "18:00"
    }
  ],
  "areaServed": ["Manchester NH","Nashua NH","Concord NH"],
  "priceRange": "$$",
  "url": "https://bobsplumbing.com"
}
</script>

Method 2: WordPress Plugins

If your site runs on WordPress, plugins like Rank Math, Yoast SEO, or Schema Pro can generate schema markup without coding. You fill in fields in a form, and the plugin generates the JSON-LD automatically.

Method 3: Google's Structured Data Markup Helper

Google provides a free tool that lets you tag elements on your existing page and generates the corresponding schema code. It's useful for beginners but produces less optimized markup than writing it manually.

How to Test Your Schema Markup

After adding schema, validate it with these free tools:

  • Google's Rich Results Test — shows whether your page is eligible for rich results
  • Schema Markup Validator — checks for syntax errors and missing fields
  • Google Search Console — the "Enhancements" section shows schema-related issues across your entire site

Common errors we see on NH business sites:

  • Missing required fields (address without postal code, hours without day specification)
  • Mismatched data (schema says "Bob's Plumbing LLC" but the GBP says "Bob's Plumbing") — keep your NAP consistent
  • Invalid JSON syntax (missing commas, unclosed brackets)
  • Using schema types that don't match the page content

Schema Markup and the Map Pack

Here's where it gets interesting for local businesses. Schema markup doesn't directly affect Google Map Pack rankings — those are driven by Google Business Profile signals. But schema markup indirectly helps by:

  • Confirming your business information — when your website's schema matches your GBP, Google has higher confidence in your listing
  • Improving click-through rate — rich results get more clicks, which sends positive signals to Google
  • Supporting entity understanding — Google builds an "entity" for your business from all available data. Schema markup is one of the clearest data sources

Why Most NH Businesses Don't Have Schema

Three reasons:

  1. Their web developer didn't know about it — many web designers focus on visual design, not technical SEO
  2. Their website builder doesn't support it easily — some platforms make it hard to add custom code
  3. They don't know it exists — schema markup is a technical SEO topic that doesn't get much mainstream attention
