Local Search Intent: How to Match What NH Customers Are Really Looking For

Alex Feb 17, 2026 10 min read

You can rank #1 for a keyword and still get zero calls. How? By ranking for the wrong intent.

Search intent is what someone actually wants when they type a query. If someone searches "plumber manchester nh," they want to hire a plumber — not read a blog post about plumbing history. If your page doesn't match what they want, they'll bounce back to Google and click your competitor instead.

Understanding and matching search intent is the difference between SEO that generates leads and SEO that just generates traffic numbers.

The 4 Types of Local Search Intent

1. Transactional Intent — "I Want to Buy/Hire"

The searcher is ready to take action. These are your highest-value keywords.

Examples:

  • "plumber near me"
  • "emergency dentist manchester nh"
  • "hvac repair nashua"
  • "best Italian restaurant concord nh"
  • "hire landscaper portsmouth"

They want: A service page with clear pricing, phone number, hours, reviews, and a way to contact you immediately.

2. Informational Intent — "I Want to Learn"

The searcher has a question. They're not ready to buy yet, but they could become a customer later.

Examples:

  • "how much does a new roof cost in nh"
  • "signs you need a new furnace"
  • "how often should you get your teeth cleaned"
  • "what is local seo"

They want: A helpful blog post or guide that answers their question thoroughly, with a soft CTA to your services.

3. Navigational Intent — "I Want to Find a Specific Business"

The searcher already knows who they're looking for.

Examples:

  • "murphy's taproom manchester"
  • "granite state dentistry hours"
  • "smith plumbing nashua phone number"

They want: Your contact info, address, hours, and directions — fast. Your Google Business Profile handles most of this.

4. Comparison Intent — "I'm Deciding Between Options"

The searcher is close to buying but comparing options first.

Examples:

  • "best plumbers in manchester nh"
  • "[your business] reviews"
  • "cheapest oil change near me"
  • "[competitor] vs [competitor]"

They want: Reviews, comparisons, case studies, and social proof. Your review strategy and review responses matter here.

How to Match Your Pages to Search Intent

Step 1: Audit Your Existing Pages

Open Google Search Console and look at the queries driving traffic to each page. Ask yourself:

  • Does the page content match what the searcher actually wants?
  • If someone searching "emergency plumber manchester nh" lands on your blog post instead of your service page, that's a mismatch
  • If someone searching "how much does seo cost" lands on your pricing page instead of an educational guide, that might also be a mismatch

Step 2: Map Keywords to Content Types

Intent Type Best Page Type Key Elements
Transactional Service page / city page Phone, form, pricing, reviews, hours, location
Informational Blog post / guide Detailed answers, images, step-by-step, expert tips
Navigational Homepage / contact page Name, address, phone, hours, directions, map
Comparison Case studies / review page Testimonials, before/after, credentials, awards

Step 3: Optimize Each Page for Its Intent

Once you know the intent, optimize accordingly:

For transactional pages (service/city pages):

  • Put the phone number above the fold
  • Include a contact form on the page
  • Show reviews and testimonials
  • List your services with clear descriptions
  • Mention pricing (at least a range) — people comparing options appreciate transparency
  • Add LocalBusiness schema

For informational pages (blog posts):

  • Answer the question in the first paragraph
  • Use clear headings that match common questions
  • Include internal links to your service pages (soft CTAs)
  • Add a "need help?" section at the end linking to your contact page
  • Write at least 1,500 words for competitive keywords

Intent Signals: How to Identify What Google Thinks

Google already knows the intent behind most queries. Check what Google shows on page 1 — that tells you what it thinks people want:

  • Map Pack showing? → Google thinks it's a local/transactional query. You need a strong Maps presence
  • Blog posts ranking? → Google thinks it's informational. Write a better blog post
  • Service pages ranking? → Google thinks it's transactional. Optimize your service page
  • "People Also Ask" boxes? → Google sees informational intent. Create FAQ content
  • Shopping results? → Google sees purchase intent. Highlight pricing and availability

The golden rule: Look at what's already ranking on page 1 for your target keyword. Create content of the same type but better.

Common Intent Mismatches (and How to Fix Them)

Mismatch 1: Blog Post Ranking for a Transactional Query

Problem: You wrote "Everything About Roof Repair" and it's ranking for "roof repair manchester nh" — but people clicking want to hire someone, not read a guide.

Fix: Create a dedicated service page optimized for "roof repair manchester nh" with contact info, pricing, and a strong CTA. Let the blog post rank for informational queries instead.

Mismatch 2: Service Page Ranking for an Informational Query

Problem: Your service page ranks for "how much does roof repair cost in nh" but people want a guide, not a sales pitch.

Fix: Write a blog post about pricing that gives real numbers and factors. Link from the blog post to your service page for people ready to hire.

Mismatch 3: Homepage Ranking for Specific Service Queries

Problem: Your homepage ranks for "ac repair portsmouth nh" instead of a dedicated AC repair page.

Fix: Create a specific service page for AC repair targeting that keyword. Use internal links from the homepage and other pages to build its authority.

Building a Content Strategy Around Intent

Use the keyword research process, but categorize every keyword by intent:

Intent-Based Content Calendar

  1. List your core services — each gets a transactional service page
  2. List your service areas — each gets a city/location page
  3. List common questions — each gets a blog post (informational)
  4. List comparison queries — create case studies and review pages
  5. Create content clusters — group related posts around a pillar page using content marketing principles

This approach ensures you have the right page type for every stage of the customer journey — from someone just learning about a problem to someone ready to hire.

Measuring Intent Match Success

Track these metrics in Search Console and Analytics:

  • Bounce rate by page: High bounce rate on a service page? Intent mismatch — people expected something different
  • Time on page: Low time on an informational post? Content isn't answering the question well enough
  • Click-through rate: Low CTR in search results? Your title/description doesn't match what people expect to find
  • Conversion rate: Traffic but no calls? The page might attract the wrong intent

Quick Wins Checklist

  • Google your top 5 service keywords — does your ranking page match the intent?
  • Check Search Console for high-impression, low-CTR pages — rewrite titles to match intent
  • Make sure every service page has a phone number and form above the fold
  • Make sure every blog post links to a relevant service page
  • Create a dedicated page for every service you offer (not just a list on one page)
  • Add FAQ sections to service pages for informational queries
  • Check that your landing pages match the ads or search queries sending traffic to them
