Website Accessibility for Local Businesses: Why It Matters for SEO

Alex Feb 17, 2026 9 min read

Website accessibility means making your website usable by everyone — including people with visual, hearing, motor, or cognitive disabilities. In the US, that's over 61 million adults.

But here's what most NH business owners don't realize: accessibility and SEO overlap significantly. The same things that make a site accessible to people with disabilities also make it easier for Google to understand and rank. It's a win-win you can't afford to ignore.

Where Accessibility and SEO Overlap

Accessibility Practice SEO Benefit
Alt text on images Google reads alt text to understand images — helps image search rankings
Proper heading hierarchy (H1→H2→H3) Helps Google understand page structure and topic hierarchy
Descriptive link text Anchor text is a key ranking signal for internal links
Video captions/transcripts Transcripts add indexable text content Google can rank
Fast page loading Page speed is a direct ranking factor
Mobile-friendly design Mobile-friendliness is a ranking factor
Clear, simple navigation Helps Google crawl and index your entire site efficiently

The Legal Side: ADA and Websites

While this isn't legal advice, you should know: ADA website accessibility lawsuits against small businesses have increased every year since 2018. Courts have increasingly ruled that websites are "places of public accommodation" under the Americans with Disabilities Act.

In New Hampshire, the risk is lower than in states like New York or California, but it's not zero. The simplest way to protect your business: make your website accessible. It's also just the right thing to do.

The standard to follow is WCAG 2.1 Level AA — the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. You don't need to be perfect, but hitting the basics covers most needs.

10 Accessibility Fixes Every NH Business Website Needs

1. Add Alt Text to Every Image

Alt text describes what an image shows. Screen readers read it aloud for blind users, and Google uses it to understand images.

<!-- Bad -->
<img src="team.jpg">

<!-- Good -->
<img src="team.jpg" alt="RankRise SEO team in Manchester NH office">

Tip: Describe the image, include relevant keywords naturally. Don't stuff keywords — "plumber manchester nh plumbing service" is spam.

2. Use Proper Heading Hierarchy

Headings should follow a logical order: one H1 per page (the main title), H2s for major sections, H3s for subsections.

  • Never skip levels (don't go from H1 to H3)
  • Never use headings just for styling — use CSS instead
  • Include keywords in headings naturally

3. Make Links Descriptive

Bad: "Click here" or "Learn more" or "Read more"

Good: "Read our complete local SEO guide for NH businesses"

Descriptive link text helps screen reader users understand where links go — and gives Google better anchor text signals for ranking.

4. Ensure Sufficient Color Contrast

Text needs to be readable against its background. The minimum contrast ratio for normal text is 4.5:1 (WCAG AA). For large text (18px+ or 14px+ bold), it's 3:1.

Common problems: light gray text on white backgrounds, colored text on colored backgrounds, text over images without an overlay.

5. Make Forms Accessible

Your contact forms and landing pages need:

  • Labels on every field — not just placeholder text (placeholders disappear when you start typing)
  • Error messages that explain what's wrong — "Please enter a valid email" not just turning the field red
  • Tab navigation — users should be able to fill out the form using only a keyboard

6. Add Keyboard Navigation

Many people can't use a mouse — they navigate entirely by keyboard (Tab, Enter, Escape, arrows). Make sure:

  • All interactive elements (links, buttons, forms) are reachable via Tab key
  • Focus indicators are visible (the outline that shows which element is selected)
  • Dropdown menus work with keyboard navigation
  • Modal dialogs can be closed with Escape

7. Use Semantic HTML

Use HTML elements for their intended purpose:

  • <nav> for navigation menus
  • <main> for the main content area
  • <footer> for the footer
  • <button> for clickable actions (not <div onclick>)
  • <table> for data tables (not for layout)

Semantic HTML helps both screen readers and Google understand your page structure.

8. Provide Text Alternatives for Media

If you have videos on your site:

  • Add captions (also helps in noisy environments and voice search)
  • Provide a text transcript (great for SEO — more indexable content)
  • Don't auto-play audio or video

9. Make Click Targets Large Enough

Buttons and links should be at least 44x44 pixels on mobile. This helps people with motor difficulties — and also improves the experience for everyone on touchscreens.

10. Test with Real Tools

Run these free checks:

  • WAVE (wave.webaim.org) — detailed accessibility audit with visual indicators
  • Google Lighthouse — built into Chrome DevTools, includes accessibility scoring
  • axe DevTools — browser extension for developers
  • Keyboard test — unplug your mouse and try to use your entire website with just the keyboard

Quick Accessibility Audit Checklist

  • Every image has descriptive alt text
  • One H1 per page, headings follow hierarchy
  • All links have descriptive text (no "click here")
  • Text contrast ratio meets 4.5:1 minimum
  • Forms have proper labels and error messages
  • All elements reachable by keyboard (Tab key)
  • Focus indicators visible on interactive elements
  • Semantic HTML used correctly
  • Videos have captions or transcripts
  • Click targets are 44x44px minimum on mobile
  • No content that flashes more than 3 times per second
  • Page language declared (<html lang="en">)

The Business Case: Accessibility = More Customers

Beyond SEO and legal protection, accessibility simply reaches more people:

  • 26% of US adults have some form of disability
  • In NH, that's roughly 350,000 potential customers
  • People with disabilities have $490 billion in disposable income annually (US total)
  • Accessible websites typically have lower bounce rates and higher conversion rates because they're easier for everyone to use

An accessible website is a better website. Period.
