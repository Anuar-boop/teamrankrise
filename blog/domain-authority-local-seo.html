What Is Domain Authority and Does It Matter for Local SEO?

By Alex Feb 17, 2026 9 min read

If you've ever talked to an SEO agency, you've probably heard "Domain Authority" thrown around like it's the most important number on the internet. DA 30, DA 50, DA 80 — but what does it actually mean? And for a local business in New Hampshire, how much should you care? The answer might surprise you.

What Domain Authority Actually Is

Domain Authority (DA) is a score created by Moz (an SEO software company) that predicts how likely a website is to rank in search results. It ranges from 1 to 100, with higher scores meaning stronger ranking potential.

Here's the critical thing most people miss: Domain Authority is not a Google metric. Google has never used DA in their ranking algorithm. It's a third-party prediction tool — useful for comparison, but not a direct ranking factor.

How DA is calculated

Moz calculates DA based primarily on:

  • Number of linking root domains — how many unique websites link to yours
  • Total number of backlinks — total links pointing to your site
  • Quality of those links — links from high-DA sites count more
  • Machine learning model — trained to correlate with Google rankings

Think of DA like a credit score for websites. It's an approximation based on available data, not an exact measurement of anything Google directly uses.

Similar metrics from other tools

Tool Metric Name Scale
Moz Domain Authority (DA) 1-100
Ahrefs Domain Rating (DR) 1-100
SEMrush Authority Score 1-100
Majestic Trust Flow 1-100

Each tool calculates differently, so a site might be DA 35 on Moz but DR 45 on Ahrefs. Don't mix scores across tools — they're not equivalent.

Typical DA Ranges for Local Businesses

If you check your DA and see a low number, don't panic. Here's what's normal:

DA Range Typical Website
1-10 Brand new websites, no backlinks yet
10-20 Small local businesses with a few citations
20-35 Established local businesses with some backlinks
35-50 Well-known local/regional businesses, active link building
50-70 Major regional businesses, news sites, mid-size companies
70-100 Major brands, news outlets, government sites (.gov)

Most NH small businesses fall in the 10-30 range — and that's completely fine for ranking in local searches. You don't need a DA of 50 to dominate your local market.

Why DA Matters Less for Local SEO Than You Think

Here's where DA gets misleading for local businesses. Local SEO rankings are determined by a different set of factors than national rankings. Google's local algorithm (used for the Map Pack and local organic results) weighs:

  1. Relevance — does your business match what the person searched?
  2. Distance — how close are you to the searcher?
  3. Prominence — reviews, citations, backlinks, and brand mentions

DA only partially captures "prominence." It completely misses relevance and distance — two factors that matter enormously in local search.

Real example: DA 15 beats DA 50

A local plumber in Manchester NH with DA 15 can easily outrank a national plumbing franchise site with DA 50 for the search "plumber Manchester NH." Why?

  • The local plumber has a Google Business Profile with a Manchester address
  • They have 40 Google reviews from Manchester customers
  • Their site has Manchester-specific content
  • They're listed on NH-specific directories

The national site has more backlinks (higher DA), but it can't match the local relevance signals. This is why local SEO is different from general SEO.

What Actually Drives Local Rankings

Instead of obsessing over DA, focus on these factors that Google actually uses for local search:

1. Google Business Profile signals

Your GBP optimization is the single biggest factor for Map Pack rankings. Category accuracy, completeness, photos, regular posts, and Q&A all matter.

2. Review signals

Quantity, quality, velocity, and diversity of Google reviews. A business with 50 recent 5-star reviews dramatically outperforms one with 5 old reviews, regardless of DA. Learn how to respond to reviews for maximum impact.

3. On-page signals

NAP consistency, location pages, LocalBusiness schema, and service-specific content. These tell Google exactly what you do and where. Follow our SEO checklist to cover all bases.

4. Citation signals

Consistent business information across directories. Quality matters more than quantity — 20 accurate citations beat 200 inconsistent ones.

5. Link signals (where DA somewhat matters)

Backlinks do matter for local SEO, but the type matters more than the quantity. One link from your local Chamber of Commerce or a New Hampshire news site is worth more than 50 links from random blogs. Focus on local link building, not chasing DA numbers.

6. Behavioral signals

Click-through rate from search results, time on site, and whether searchers contact you. Building trust and having a mobile-friendly site directly impacts these metrics.

When DA Does Matter

DA isn't useless. Here's when it's actually helpful:

Competitive analysis

Comparing your DA to direct competitors gives a rough sense of the competitive landscape. If all competitors in your market have DA 25-30 and you're at DA 10, you know link building needs attention. Our competitor analysis guide shows how to use this data.

Evaluating backlink opportunities

When deciding whether a potential link is worth pursuing, DA of the linking site is one useful signal (among many). A link from a DA 40 local news site is generally more valuable than one from a DA 5 personal blog.

Measuring progress over time

Tracking your own DA quarterly shows whether your link building efforts are working. A steady climb from DA 12 to DA 22 over a year suggests your authority is genuinely growing.

Red Flags: When Someone Overemphasizes DA

Be cautious if an SEO agency:

  • Guarantees a specific DA number — DA is calculated by Moz, not Google. No one controls it directly.
  • Sells "high DA" backlinks — this is often a link scheme that can get you penalized. Google explicitly warns against buying links.
  • Uses DA as the only metric — a good SEO strategy looks at rankings, traffic, conversions, and revenue — not just DA.
  • Says you need DA 50+ to rank locally — plenty of DA 15-25 sites rank #1 for local keywords. See our guide on why local businesses don't rank — DA is rarely the reason.

How to Improve Your Domain Authority (the Right Way)

If you want to raise your DA, the good news is that the right strategies also improve your actual rankings:

  1. Create content worth linking to — helpful, detailed guides that other sites want to reference. Your content calendar should include linkable assets.
  2. Build local backlinks — chamber directories, local sponsorships, press mentions. Follow our NH-specific backlink guide.
  3. Guest post on industry sites — contribute expert content to relevant blogs or publications
  4. Get listed in quality directories — citations that include links count toward DA
  5. Fix broken links — use Google Search Console to find and fix any 404 errors

Expect slow, steady growth. Going from DA 10 to DA 20 might take 6-12 months of consistent effort. But the traffic and rankings you gain along the way are the real reward.

The Bottom Line

Domain Authority is a useful benchmark — think of it as a health indicator, not a goal in itself. For local local businesses, your time is better spent on:

  1. Optimizing your Google Business Profile
  2. Getting genuine customer reviews
  3. Creating helpful, location-specific content
  4. Building consistent local citations
  5. Earning a few quality local backlinks

Do those five things well, and the rankings will come — regardless of what your DA score says.
