5 Signs Your Website Is Losing You Customers

Alex Feb 17, 2026 9 min read

Your website might look fine to you. Maybe you had it built a few years ago, it loads okay on your laptop, and the phone number is right there on the homepage.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: your website could be silently turning away dozens of potential customers every month — and you'd never know it.

There's no notification that says "a customer in Manchester searched for your service, found your site, and left because it took 6 seconds to load." They just... leave. And go to your competitor instead.

After auditing hundreds of small business websites across New Hampshire, here are the 5 most common problems we see — and exactly how to fix each one.

1. Your Site Doesn't Show Up on Google

This is the biggest one. If you Google your business name and it shows up — great. But that's not the test that matters.

The test that matters is: what happens when someone searches for what you do?

Try this right now:

  1. Open Google in an incognito/private window
  2. Search for "[your service] [your city]" (e.g., "plumber Nashua NH" or "dentist Concord NH")
  3. Look at the first page of results and the Map Pack (the map with 3 businesses)

If you're not there, you're invisible to the vast majority of potential customers. 68% of online experiences start with a search engine, and 75% of people never scroll past page 1.

Why This Happens

  • No SEO strategy — your site was built to look nice, not to rank
  • Missing meta descriptions and title tags — Google doesn't know what your pages are about
  • No structured data — Google can't verify your business details for Map Pack results
  • Thin content — not enough text for Google to understand your expertise

How to Fix It

Start with the basics: make sure every page has a unique title tag (under 60 characters) that includes your service and city. Add a meta description (under 160 characters) that tells searchers exactly what you offer. If you haven't claimed and optimized your Google Business Profile, do that today — it's free and it's the single fastest way to appear in local search results.

2. Your Website Is Slow (Especially on Mobile)

Here's a stat that should worry you: 53% of mobile visitors leave a site that takes longer than 3 seconds to load. Three seconds.

Most small business websites we audit in NH take 5-8 seconds to load on mobile. That means over half of your potential customers are gone before they see a single word on your site.

Common Speed Killers

  • Uncompressed images — a single hero image can be 3-5MB when it should be 200KB
  • Too many plugins — WordPress sites with 30+ plugins are painfully slow
  • No caching — your server rebuilds the page from scratch for every visitor
  • Cheap hosting — shared hosting with hundreds of other sites on the same server
  • Render-blocking resources — CSS and JavaScript files that prevent the page from displaying

How to Fix It

Test your site speed at Google PageSpeed Insights (pagespeed.web.dev). It's free and tells you exactly what's slowing you down. The quick wins: compress your images (use WebP format), enable browser caching, and remove any plugins you're not actively using. If your site scores below 50 on mobile, speed optimization should be your #1 priority.

3. Your Site Isn't Mobile-Friendly

In 2026, over 60% of Google searches come from mobile devices. For local searches like "restaurant near me" or "emergency plumber," that number is even higher — closer to 80%.

Google uses "mobile-first indexing," which means it primarily looks at the mobile version of your site to determine rankings. If your site doesn't work well on a phone, Google pushes you down in search results — even for people searching on desktops.

Signs Your Site Isn't Mobile-Friendly

  • Text is too small to read without pinching and zooming
  • Buttons and links are too close together to tap accurately
  • The page requires horizontal scrolling
  • Navigation menu doesn't work on phones (no hamburger menu)
  • Forms are impossible to fill out on a small screen
  • Missing the viewport meta tag entirely

How to Fix It

Pull out your phone right now and visit your own website. Try to do what a customer would do: find your phone number, read about your services, fill out a contact form. If any of that is frustrating, your customers feel the same way. A responsive redesign is the fix — the site should automatically adapt to any screen size. Every page needs a <meta name="viewport"> tag, and touch targets should be at least 48x48 pixels.

4. You Have No Clear Call-to-Action

Imagine a customer finds your site. They're interested. They want to hire you. Now what?

On too many small business websites, the answer is "hunt around and hope you find a phone number or contact form." That's not good enough. Every page on your website should make it obvious what the visitor should do next.

What We See on Struggling Sites

  • Phone number buried in the footer (or missing entirely)
  • No contact form — just a "mailto:" link that opens their email app
  • Contact page that's 3 clicks deep in the navigation
  • "Contact Us" is the only call-to-action (too generic, no urgency)
  • No value proposition — why should they choose YOU over the next result?

How to Fix It

Put your phone number in the top navigation — clickable on mobile. Add a strong call-to-action button above the fold on every page. Use specific language: not "Contact Us" but "Get Your Free Estimate" or "Book an Appointment Today." And make sure your contact form is short — name, email, phone, and a message field. Nothing more. Every extra field you add reduces form completions by about 10%.

5. Your Content Is Outdated or Missing

When was the last time you updated your website? If you have a copyright date of 2022 in the footer, visitors assume you might be out of business. If your blog's last post was from 2023, Google assumes your site is stale.

Fresh, relevant content is one of the strongest signals Google uses to rank websites. Businesses that blog regularly get 55% more website visitors than those that don't.

Content Red Flags

  • Homepage hasn't been updated in over a year
  • Services page lists things you no longer offer (or doesn't list new ones)
  • No blog or resource section at all
  • Pages with only 50-100 words of text (Google considers this "thin content")
  • Stock photos from 2015 that look nothing like your actual business
  • No mention of your city, town, or service area

How to Fix It

Update your copyright year (small but matters). Make sure every service you offer has its own page with at least 500 words of unique content. Start a blog — even one post per month makes a difference. Write about topics your customers actually ask about. A Nashua plumber could write about "How to Prevent Frozen Pipes in NH Winters." A Portsmouth restaurant could write about "Best Seasonal Ingredients from New Hampshire Farms." This kind of content builds topical authority and brings in search traffic for months or years.

The Cost of Doing Nothing

These aren't just theoretical problems. They have a real dollar cost. Let's do some quick math:

  • Say 500 people per month search for your service in your area
  • If you're not on page 1, you get roughly 0% of those clicks
  • Even a modest 10% click-through rate would bring 50 visitors/month
  • If 5% of those visitors become customers, that's 2-3 new customers per month
  • For most service businesses, each customer is worth $200-$2,000+

That's $400 to $6,000+ in revenue you're leaving on the table every single month. Over a year, fixing your website could mean the difference between struggling and thriving.

Which Fix Should You Prioritize?

If you're dealing with multiple issues (most businesses are), here's the order we recommend:

  1. Mobile-friendliness — this affects everything else
  2. Page speed — slow sites lose visitors before they see your content
  3. Google Business Profile — free, and delivers fast results for local search
  4. On-page SEO — title tags, meta descriptions, structured data
  5. Content — ongoing optimization that compounds over time

The businesses that fix these issues see results within 60-90 days — sometimes sooner. The ones that wait fall further behind every month as competitors invest in their online presence.
