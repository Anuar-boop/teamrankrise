How Much Does SEO Cost for a Small Business in NH? (2026 Pricing Guide)

By Alex February 17, 2026 8 min read

If you own a small business in New Hampshire and you've started looking into SEO, you've probably noticed something frustrating: nobody wants to give you a straight answer on price. One agency quotes you $5,000 a month. Another says they can do it for $299. A freelancer on Fiverr offers "full SEO" for $50. And you're left wondering — what does SEO actually cost, and what should I be paying?

It's a fair question. SEO pricing is notoriously opaque, and the range is enormous. For most New Hampshire business owners, the confusion alone is enough to make them put off SEO entirely — which means they keep losing customers to competitors who figured it out first.

This guide is going to give you real numbers. Not vague ranges. Not "it depends" non-answers. We'll break down exactly what SEO costs at every level, what you should expect to get for your money, and the red flags that mean you're about to waste it.

The Quick Answer

For a local New Hampshire small business — a plumber, dentist, restaurant, law firm, landscaper, or retail shop serving one or a few towns — expect to pay $500 to $2,500 per month for professional SEO from a reputable agency or consultant.

That's the honest range for quality local SEO work in 2026. Here's why:

  • Anything under $300/month is almost certainly templated, automated work. You'll get a monthly report full of vanity metrics and very little actual improvement in your rankings. These providers are running the same playbook for hundreds of clients with zero customization for your business or market
  • $500 to $2,500/month is the sweet spot where you get a real strategist looking at your specific situation, creating custom content, optimizing your Google Business Profile, building real local citations, and making technical fixes to your website
  • Anything over $3,000/month is typically enterprise-level SEO designed for multi-location businesses, e-commerce sites, or companies competing in national markets. If you're a single-location bakery in Keene or a plumber in Manchester, you don't need this

Now let's dig into what actually drives these numbers.

What Affects SEO Pricing

SEO isn't a one-size-fits-all product, which is why the pricing varies so much. Four main factors determine what you'll pay:

1. Competition level in your market. A plumber in Manchester, NH is competing against dozens of other plumbing companies, many of which are already investing in SEO. Getting to page one in a competitive market takes more work — more content, more link building, more technical optimization. A specialty bakery in Keene, on the other hand, might face only a handful of competitors online, making it significantly easier (and cheaper) to rank.

2. The current state of your website. If your website was built in 2018 on a free Wix template and hasn't been touched since, it needs a lot more work than a modern, well-structured site that just needs some optimization. Some businesses need a complete website rebuild before SEO can even begin. Others just need meta tags fixed, page speed improved, and content added. The starting point matters enormously. Not sure where your site stands? Our guide to the 5 signs your website is losing customers is a quick way to assess.

3. Your goals. There's a significant difference between "I want to show up on page one for 3 keywords in my town" and "I want to dominate search results across the entire Seacoast region for 20+ service keywords." Bigger goals require more content creation, more link building, and more ongoing optimization — all of which cost more.

4. Agency expertise and overhead. A solo freelancer working from their home office in Concord has different costs than a 15-person agency with offices in Boston. Both can deliver results, but their pricing structures will reflect their overhead. More expensive doesn't always mean better, and cheaper doesn't always mean worse — but you should understand what you're paying for.

One-Time vs. Monthly

Some agencies offer one-time SEO audits or setup packages ranging from $500 to $3,000. These can be valuable if your website just needs a tune-up rather than ongoing management. But SEO is not a one-and-done task — Google's algorithm changes constantly, competitors are always optimizing, and fresh content needs to be created regularly. Most businesses see the best results with ongoing monthly SEO.

What You Should Get for Your Money

Regardless of what you're paying, your SEO provider should be delivering tangible, measurable work every month. Here's what a legitimate local SEO engagement for an NH business should include:

Monthly reporting with real metrics. Not just "impressions" and "reach" — those are vanity numbers. You should see keyword rankings (which keywords you're ranking for and where), organic traffic numbers, phone calls and form submissions from organic search, and Google Business Profile views and actions. If your SEO company sends you a PDF with pretty graphs but you can't tell whether it's actually working, that's a problem.

Google Business Profile optimization. Your GBP is the single most important asset for local SEO. Your provider should be regularly updating your profile with new photos, posts, Q&A responses, service descriptions, and category optimizations. They should be monitoring and responding to reviews, and ensuring your business information is accurate and complete.

On-page SEO. This means optimizing your website's meta titles, meta descriptions, heading tags, image alt text, internal linking structure, and page content. Every service page should target specific keywords relevant to your business and location. If your SEO company hasn't touched your website's actual content, they're not doing on-page SEO.

Technical audit and fixes. Your website needs to load fast, be mobile-friendly, have clean URL structures, proper schema markup, no broken links, and a working XML sitemap. A good SEO provider will run a technical audit in the first month and fix critical issues promptly.

Content strategy and creation. Fresh, useful content is one of the most reliable ways to improve your search rankings over time. This could mean blog posts targeting questions your customers ask, location pages for towns you serve, service pages with detailed information, or FAQ content. At a minimum, your SEO provider should be publishing 2-4 pieces of quality content per month.

Local citation building. Your business needs to be listed consistently across dozens of directories — Google, Bing, Yelp, Apple Maps, BBB, your local Chamber of Commerce, and industry-specific directories. Your provider should be building and managing these citations, ensuring your Name, Address, and Phone number (NAP) are identical everywhere.

Red Flags: When to Walk Away

The SEO industry, unfortunately, has more than its share of scam artists and incompetent providers. Here are the warning signs that should make you walk away immediately:

"We guarantee #1 rankings." Nobody — absolutely nobody — can guarantee a specific ranking on Google. Google's algorithm uses over 200 ranking factors and changes constantly. Any company that guarantees a #1 position is either lying or planning to use risky tactics that could get your site penalized. Legitimate SEO providers will tell you they'll work to improve your rankings and can show you past results, but they won't guarantee a specific position.

Long-term contracts with no performance benchmarks. Some agencies lock you into 12 or 24-month contracts with no clear performance goals. If they're not willing to set measurable benchmarks — and let you walk if those benchmarks aren't met — they're not confident in their ability to deliver results. Look for month-to-month agreements or contracts with clear performance clauses.

No transparency about what they're doing. If you ask your SEO company "what did you do this month?" and the answer is vague hand-waving about "optimizations" and "improvements," you're paying for nothing. A legitimate provider will show you exactly what work was completed: which pages were optimized, what content was created, which citations were built, what technical issues were fixed.

Using "proprietary tools" as an excuse for vagueness. Some agencies claim they use "proprietary technology" or "secret methods" to justify why they can't tell you what they're doing. This is almost always a smokescreen for automated, templated work — or worse, no work at all. The fundamentals of SEO are well-established and documented. There's no secret sauce that a legitimate agency would need to hide from you.

Buying links from spammy sites. Link building is a legitimate part of SEO, but the way it's done matters enormously. If your SEO provider is buying links from low-quality directories, link farms, or irrelevant foreign websites, they're putting your site at serious risk of a Google penalty. Ask your provider where they're building links and review the sites. Links should come from relevant, legitimate websites — local newspapers, chambers of commerce, industry directories, and complementary businesses.
